**Character.AI** (legally Character Technologies, Inc.) is an American [artificial intelligence](/wiki/artificial_intelligence) company that operates a [generative AI](/index.php?title=Generative_artificial_intelligence&action=edit&redlink=1) chatbot platform allowing users to create, customize, and interact with AI-powered characters. Founded in November 2021 by [Noam Shazeer](/index.php?title=Noam_Shazeer&action=edit&redlink=1) and Daniel De Freitas, both former [Google](/index.php?title=Google&action=edit&redlink=1) researchers, the company is headquartered in Menlo Park, California. Character.AI became one of the most popular consumer AI applications, particularly among teenagers, before becoming the center of significant safety controversies related to its effects on minors. In August 2024, [Google](/index.php?title=Google&action=edit&redlink=1) signed a $2.7 billion licensing deal with the company and hired back both co-founders to work on [Gemini](/index.php?title=Gemini_(language_model)&action=edit&redlink=1).

## History

### Founding (2021)

Character.AI was established in November 2021 by Noam Shazeer and Daniel De Freitas, both of whom had worked extensively on AI-related projects at [Google](/index.php?title=Google&action=edit&redlink=1).

**[Noam Shazeer](/index.php?title=Noam_Shazeer&action=edit&redlink=1)** is one of the most influential researchers in modern AI. He was a lead author on the seminal 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need," which introduced the [Transformer](/index.php?title=Transformer_(machine_learning_model)&action=edit&redlink=1) architecture that underpins virtually all modern [large language models](/wiki/large_language_model), including [GPT-4](/wiki/gpt-4), [Claude](/wiki/claude), and [Gemini](/index.php?title=Gemini_(language_model)&action=edit&redlink=1). At Google, Shazeer worked on numerous AI projects and was instrumental in the development of conversational AI systems.

**Daniel De Freitas** was the lead designer of an experimental conversational AI at Google initially called Meena, which was later renamed [LaMDA](/index.php?title=LaMDA&action=edit&redlink=1) (Language Model for Dialogue Applications). LaMDA became the foundation for [Google Bard](/index.php?title=Google_Bard&action=edit&redlink=1) and, subsequently, [Gemini](/index.php?title=Gemini_(language_model)&action=edit&redlink=1).

Both Shazeer and De Freitas grew frustrated with Google's cautious approach to releasing conversational AI technology to the public. Despite personal pleas from [Google](/index.php?title=Google&action=edit&redlink=1) CEO [Sundar Pichai](/index.php?title=Sundar_Pichai&action=edit&redlink=1) to stay, the duo left the company in late 2021 to pursue their vision of making conversational AI widely available. The company raised $43 million in seed funding at the time of its founding.

### Beta Launch and Early Growth (2022)

The first beta version of Character.AI's platform was made publicly available on September 16, 2022. The platform allowed users to create AI-powered "characters" by defining their personalities, setting specific behavioral parameters, and publishing them for the community to interact with. Users could engage in conversations with these characters, which ranged from fictional personas to representations of historical figures and celebrities.

Early adoption was rapid. The Washington Post reported in October 2022 that the site had logged "hundreds of thousands of user interactions" in its first three weeks of beta testing. By December 2022, just three months after the beta launch, the platform was generating approximately 1 billion words per day, demonstrating extraordinary user engagement.

The platform's appeal lay in its open-ended conversational capabilities. Unlike task-oriented AI assistants like [ChatGPT](/wiki/chatgpt) or [Claude](/wiki/claude), Character.AI was designed primarily for entertainment, creativity, and social interaction. Users could:

- Create custom characters with defined personalities and backstories
- Engage in role-playing scenarios
- Play text-adventure games managed by AI characters
- Have conversations with AI versions of famous people
- Create group chats with multiple AI characters

### Series A Funding and Unicorn Status (2023)

In March 2023, Character.AI closed a $150 million Series A funding round led by [Andreessen Horowitz](/index.php?title=Andreessen_Horowitz&action=edit&redlink=1) (a16z), with participation from previous investors including Nat Friedman (former [GitHub](/index.php?title=GitHub&action=edit&redlink=1) CEO), Elad Gil, SV Angel, and A Capital. The round valued the company at approximately $1 billion, establishing Character.AI as a [unicorn](/index.php?title=Unicorn_(finance)&action=edit&redlink=1). Andreessen Horowitz General Partner Sarah Wang joined Character.AI's board of directors.

### Mobile Launch and Peak Growth (2023-2024)

In May 2023, Character.AI released mobile apps for both iOS and Android. The launch was immediately successful: the app received over 1.7 million downloads within its first week, placing it among the most popular AI applications at the time.

User growth continued to accelerate through 2023 and into 2024. The platform reached approximately 28 million monthly active users at its peak in mid-2024. Key user engagement metrics from this period:

| Metric | Value | Period |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Monthly Active Users (peak) | ~28 million | Mid-2024 |
| Average Session Duration | ~29 minutes | 2024 |
| Daily Words Generated | 1 billion+ | Late 2022 onward |
| App Downloads (first week) | 1.7 million+ | May 2023 |
| Monthly Active Users | ~20 million | Early 2025 |

A notable demographic characteristic of Character.AI's user base was its heavy concentration among teenagers. The platform became particularly popular with users under 18, who used it for creative expression, entertainment, emotional support, and social interaction. This demographic profile would later become central to the company's most significant controversies.

### Revenue and Business Model

Character.AI adopted a freemium model, offering the core chat experience for free while monetizing through a premium subscription called **c.ai+**, priced at $9.99 per month (or $94.99 per year, roughly $7.92/month). The subscription offered:

| Feature | Free Tier | c.ai+ |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Basic chat | Yes | Yes |
| Priority access during peak times | No | Yes |
| Faster response speeds | No | Yes |
| Voice calls with AI characters | No | Yes |
| Early access to new features | No | Yes |
| Group chat rooms | Limited | Full access |

In 2024, the company also began generating advertising revenue by selling ads from brands like Yelp and Webtoon within the platform's social feed. Despite the large user base, revenue growth was modest relative to the platform's scale. The company generated $32.2 million in annual revenue in 2024, a 112% increase from $15.2 million in 2023, with projections targeting $50.1 million for 2025.

## Google Licensing Deal (2024)

### Background

In August 2024, Google signed a $2.7 billion agreement with Character.AI that fundamentally reshaped both companies. The deal was structured to avoid regulatory scrutiny as a formal acquisition: rather than purchasing Character.AI outright, Google paid for a non-exclusive license to use the startup's generative AI technology.

### Key Terms

As part of the agreement:

- **Technology License**: Google received a non-exclusive license to Character.AI's generative AI models and technology.
- **Founder Return**: Both co-founders, Noam Shazeer and Daniel De Freitas, returned to Google along with certain members of the research team. Shazeer was appointed co-lead for Google's flagship AI model [Gemini](/index.php?title=Gemini_(language_model)&action=edit&redlink=1), while De Freitas joined [Google DeepMind](/wiki/google_deepmind) as a research scientist.
- **Company Independence**: Character.AI remained a nominally independent company, continuing to operate its platform with a new leadership team.

### Leadership Transition

Following the departure of the co-founders, Dominic Perella, the company's general counsel, became interim CEO. In June 2025, Karandeep Anand was appointed as the permanent CEO, and Perella transitioned to the role of Chief Legal Officer and SVP of Global Operations.

### Antitrust Scrutiny

The deal drew scrutiny from the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ), which investigated whether the arrangement was structured to circumvent regulatory oversight for what was effectively an acquisition. Regulators expressed concern that similar "licensing plus acqui-hire" deal structures could be used by large technology companies to absorb competitive threats from smaller, innovative companies without triggering formal merger review processes. The investigation was part of a broader regulatory trend examining the growing influence of large tech companies over the AI startup ecosystem.

## Safety Controversies

Character.AI became the subject of multiple lawsuits and regulatory investigations related to the safety of its platform for minors. The controversies centered on allegations that the platform's chatbots contributed to psychological harm and, in the most serious cases, teen suicides.

### The Sewell Setzer III Case

The most prominent case involved Sewell Setzer III, a 14-year-old from Florida who died by suicide in February 2024 after developing a deep emotional attachment to a Character.AI chatbot. His mother, Megan Garcia, filed a lawsuit against Character.AI in October 2024, alleging that the platform's AI characters had encouraged harmful behavior and that the company had failed to implement adequate safeguards for minors.

The lawsuit alleged that the teenager had spent months engaging in extended conversations with an AI character modeled after Daenerys Targaryen from Game of Thrones, and that these interactions had become increasingly emotional and isolating. The case received widespread media coverage and catalyzed a broader public debate about the responsibilities of AI companies in protecting young users.

### Additional Lawsuits

A wave of additional lawsuits followed, with multiple families alleging that Character.AI's chatbots had:

- Contributed to mental health deterioration among teenagers
- Exposed minors to sexually explicit content
- Failed to detect and intervene when users expressed suicidal ideation
- Provided inadequate safeguards against emotional manipulation

A key legal argument in several of these cases was that Shazeer and De Freitas had specifically left Google to found Character.AI as a way to bypass Google's more stringent safety protocols. A judge in one case ruled that the complaint had sufficiently alleged this claim, allowing the case to proceed.

### Settlement with Google

In January 2026, Google and Character.AI agreed to settle the multiple lawsuits filed by families whose children died by suicide or experienced psychological harm allegedly linked to AI chatbots. The settlement terms were not fully disclosed.

### Safety Measures and Restrictions

In response to the lawsuits and growing public pressure, Character.AI implemented a series of safety measures, particularly for users under 18:

| Safety Measure | Description | Date Implemented |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Separate teen model | A distinct AI model for users under 18 that limits romantic and sensitive content | 2024 |
| Self-harm intervention | Detection of self-harm or suicidal language with redirection to helplines | 2024 |
| Session time notifications | Alerts after one-hour sessions, with stricter limits for minors | 2024 |
| Content disclaimers | Prominent reminders that AI characters are not real people | 2024 |
| Parental controls | Dashboard for parents showing time spent and characters interacted with | Q1 2025 |
| Open-ended chat ban for minors | Users under 18 banned from open-ended conversations with AI personas | November 2025 |

The most significant restriction came in October 2025, when Character.AI announced it would ban users under 18 from engaging in open-ended chats with its AI personas. By November 25, 2025, teens were no longer able to message AI characters in open-ended conversations. The company worked to build an alternative under-18 experience focused on creative activities such as creating videos, stories, and streams with AI characters.

## Technology

Character.AI developed its own proprietary large language models optimized for conversational interaction. Unlike general-purpose models like [GPT-4](/wiki/gpt-4) or [Claude](/wiki/claude), Character.AI's models were designed to maintain character consistency, generate engaging dialogue, and support long-form role-playing scenarios.

The platform's key technical differentiators included:

- **Character Consistency**: Models could maintain a character's personality, backstory, and behavioral traits across extended conversations.
- **Multi-Character Interaction**: Support for group chats involving multiple AI characters simultaneously.
- **User-Defined Characters**: A character creation system allowing non-technical users to define AI personalities through natural language descriptions.
- **Voice Capabilities**: AI characters could engage in voice calls with users, expanding interactions beyond text.

After the Google licensing deal, the company shifted away from exclusive reliance on proprietary models and began incorporating open-source alternatives into its platform.

## Impact and Cultural Significance

Character.AI's success demonstrated a previously unproven market for entertainment-focused conversational AI. While most AI companies in 2022 and 2023 focused on productivity, coding, and enterprise use cases, Character.AI proved that millions of users, particularly younger demographics, were eager to use AI for social interaction, creative expression, and emotional connection.

The platform's popularity among teenagers raised fundamental questions about:

- The ethical responsibilities of AI companies when their products are used by minors
- The psychological effects of extended AI companionship on developing minds
- The adequacy of existing regulatory frameworks for governing AI-powered social platforms
- The potential for AI systems to cross boundaries from helpful tool to harmful dependency

These questions remain actively debated as of early 2026, with legislators in multiple countries considering new regulations specifically targeting AI platforms used by minors.
