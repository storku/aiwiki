**Devin** is an autonomous [artificial intelligence](/wiki/artificial_intelligence) software engineering agent developed by **Cognition AI** (also known as Cognition Labs), a San Francisco-based AI startup. Marketed as "the world's first AI software engineer," Devin is designed to plan, write, debug, test, and deploy code end-to-end with minimal human oversight, operating inside a sandboxed cloud environment equipped with its own shell, code editor, and web browser.[&#91;1&#93;](#cite_note-1)[&#91;2&#93;](#cite_note-2) Unlike code-completion tools such as [GitHub Copilot](/wiki/github_copilot), which provide inline suggestions inside a developer's IDE, Devin functions as a fully autonomous agent capable of executing multi-step engineering tasks across an entire software development lifecycle.[&#91;3&#93;](#cite_note-3)

Cognition AI announced Devin on March 12, 2024, emerging from stealth mode. The announcement generated intense interest across the technology industry, drawing both praise from investors and anxiety from software engineers concerned about job displacement.[&#91;4&#93;](#cite_note-4) The company subsequently raised over $696 million in total funding across multiple rounds, reaching a post-money valuation of $10.2 billion by September 2025.[&#91;5&#93;](#cite_note-5)

## Cognition AI

### Company overview

Cognition AI is an applied [artificial intelligence](/wiki/artificial_intelligence) startup headquartered in San Francisco, California. The company was founded in August 2023 with a focus on building AI systems with advanced reasoning capabilities. Cognition describes itself as "an applied AI lab focused on reasoning" and positions Devin not merely as a coding tool but as a collaborative AI teammate for engineering organizations.[&#91;6&#93;](#cite_note-6)

As of early 2026, Cognition employs approximately 272 people, having grown rapidly from a team of around 10 engineers at the time of Devin's announcement in March 2024. A significant portion of this growth came through the company's acquisition of Windsurf (formerly Codeium) in July 2025, which added roughly 210 employees to the organization.[&#91;7&#93;](#cite_note-7)[&#91;8&#93;](#cite_note-8)

### Revenue growth

Cognition's revenue growth has been described by analysts as among the fastest in the agentic coding segment. The company's annual recurring revenue (ARR) grew from roughly $1 million in September 2024 to approximately $73 million by June 2025, representing roughly a 73-fold increase over nine months. Cognition has also reported keeping cumulative net burn under $20 million since its founding, an unusually capital-efficient profile for a company growing at this pace.[&#91;13&#93;](#cite_note-13)

Following the acquisition of Windsurf in July 2025 (which brought approximately $82 million in ARR and over 350 enterprise customers), the combined entity's ARR reached the $150 million range. ### Enterprise customers

Devin has been adopted by several major enterprises, including Goldman Sachs, Citi, Dell, Cisco, Palantir, Nubank, and Mercado Libre. Goldman Sachs became the first major bank to deploy Devin, with CIO Marco Argenti describing it as "our new employee" who would "start doing stuff on the behalf of our developers."[&#91;14&#93;](#cite_note-14)[&#91;15&#93;](#cite_note-15)

### Acquisition of Windsurf

In July 2025, Cognition acquired Windsurf (formerly Codeium), an AI-powered IDE and coding platform, for $250 million. The acquisition was notable for the speed at which it came together: the first call between the companies occurred after 5 PM on Friday, July 11, and by Monday morning, July 14, the deal was announced.[&#91;8&#93;](#cite_note-8)

The acquisition followed a dramatic sequence of events. OpenAI had offered approximately $3 billion to acquire Windsurf, but the deal expired. Hours later, Google executed a $2.4 billion reverse-acquihire, hiring away Windsurf's CEO Varun Mohan, co-founder Douglas Chen, and several research leaders. Cognition then stepped in to acquire the remaining entity, including Windsurf's intellectual property, product, trademark, brand, approximately 210 remaining employees, and the $82 million ARR business with 350+ enterprise customers.[&#91;8&#93;](#cite_note-8)[&#91;16&#93;](#cite_note-16)

In the near term, Windsurf's team continues developing its AI-powered IDE while Cognition works on Devin. Cognition has stated plans to eventually integrate Windsurf's IP and capabilities into its own products.[&#91;8&#93;](#cite_note-8)

## Announcement and reception

### March 2024 announcement

On March 12, 2024, Cognition AI emerged from stealth mode with a blog post and social media campaign introducing Devin as "the first AI software engineer." The announcement included several demonstration videos showing Devin autonomously completing engineering tasks, passing technical interviews at leading AI companies, earning money by completing real freelance jobs on Upwork, and achieving a new state-of-the-art score on the [SWE-bench](/wiki/swe-bench) coding benchmark.[&#91;1&#93;](#cite_note-1)[&#91;4&#93;](#cite_note-4)

The announcement quickly went viral on X (formerly Twitter) and generated widespread media coverage. Aravind Srinivas, CEO of Perplexity.ai, praised Devin, stating it "seemed to be 'the first demo of any agent, leave alone coding, that seems to cross the threshold' of human capability." The demonstration attracted attention from investors, tech executives, and developers around the world.[&#91;4&#93;](#cite_note-4)

### Mixed reactions

The software engineering community responded with a wide range of emotions. Some developers expressed genuine excitement about the potential for AI to handle tedious or repetitive coding tasks. Others reacted with fear, anxiety, or anger, particularly in the context of ongoing tech industry layoffs throughout 2023 and 2024. Discourse around the tool frequently centered on concerns that it might replace engineers and eliminate entry-level software development jobs.[&#91;4&#93;](#cite_note-4)[&#91;17&#93;](#cite_note-17)

### Skepticism and debunking

Devin's promotional materials came under significant scrutiny in the weeks following the announcement. In April 2024, Carl Brown, a software developer who runs the YouTube channel "Internet of Bugs," published a detailed video titled "Debunking Devin: 'First AI Software Engineer' Upwork lie exposed!" The video analyzed one of the Upwork job demonstrations that Cognition had showcased and identified several issues:[&#91;18&#93;](#cite_note-18)[&#91;19&#93;](#cite_note-19)

- **Task mismatch**: The Upwork customer had asked for setup instructions (documentation), not code. Devin instead wrote, tested, and debugged code that was not what the customer requested.
- **Fabricated errors**: A file that Devin appeared to fix during the demonstration was not actually present in the original repository. Devin had created the file itself and then "fixed" errors in its own creation, some of which were nonsensical errors that no human developer would make.
- **Efficiency disparity**: The entire solution took Devin many hours to produce, while Brown noted he was able to answer the customer's actual question in approximately 30 minutes.

The Hacker News discussion of the debunking video reached the front page and received hundreds of comments. Additional YouTube channels, including Computer Vision Project, published their own analyses corroborating the criticisms.[&#91;19&#93;](#cite_note-19)[&#91;20&#93;](#cite_note-20)

## Capabilities

### Core functionality

Devin is designed to function as an autonomous software engineering agent that can handle entire development workflows independently. When a user provides a task in natural language (for example, "Build a website that simulates the Game of Life" or "Fix this bug described in this GitHub issue"), Devin's Planner module analyzes the request and breaks it down into a detailed, step-by-step plan. After the user approves the plan, Devin proceeds to execute it in its sandboxed environment, writing code, running tests, and iterating on failures with visible progress indicators.[&#91;2&#93;](#cite_note-2)[&#91;21&#93;](#cite_note-21)

Key capabilities include:

| Capability | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Autonomous planning | Breaks complex tasks into hundreds of subtasks and executes them in sequence, adapting to obstacles |
| Code writing and editing | Writes, modifies, and refactors code across multiple programming languages and frameworks |
| Bug detection and fixing | Scans codebases, detects bugs or syntax errors, and applies fixes automatically |
| Test generation | Creates and runs test suites, iterating until tests pass |
| Environment setup | Configures development environments, installs dependencies, and manages build systems |
| Web browsing and research | Uses a built-in browser to read documentation, search Stack Overflow, and research APIs |
| Code migration | Handles framework or library migrations (e.g., upgrading React versions, converting Python 2 to Python 3) |
| Pull request creation | Independently creates PRs, responds to PR comments, and reviews other PRs |
| Learning from feedback | Recalls relevant context from previous interactions and improves over time |
| Deployment | Can deploy applications to various platforms and handle CI/CD pipeline configurations |

### Sandboxed environment

Devin operates within a self-contained, sandboxed cloud compute environment. Each Devin session runs in an isolated container that includes three essential developer tools: a shell (command line), a code editor, and a web browser. This sandboxed design provides several advantages:[&#91;2&#93;](#cite_note-2)[&#91;22&#93;](#cite_note-22)

- **Isolation**: Devin cannot access production databases unless a user explicitly provides credentials (which Cognition discourages).
- **Parallelization**: Multiple sandboxed Devin sessions can run side-by-side without interfering with each other.
- **Iterative debugging**: When tests fail or linters report errors, Devin can iterate autonomously within its sandbox until the build turns green.
- **Reproducibility**: All sessions are recorded, allowing users to review exactly what Devin did.

### Memory and context management

Beneath the workspace sits a memory layer that stores vectorized snapshots of the codebase along with a full replay timeline of every command, file diff, and browser tab that Devin touches during a session. This enables Devin to maintain awareness of the full project context and recall relevant information from earlier in a session.[&#91;22&#93;](#cite_note-22)

### Integrations

Devin integrates with several widely used development and communication tools:

| Integration | Functionality |
|-------------|--------------|
| GitHub | Creates and reviews PRs, responds to PR comments, works from GitHub issues |
| Slack | Users can trigger Devin with @Devin in any channel; receives status notifications via direct message |
| Jira | Can open and work on Jira tickets, broadcasting PR status to Slack |
| VS Code | IDE extension for initiating Devin sessions and reviewing work |
| API | Programmatic access for custom integrations |

Teams typically set up a dedicated Slack channel (such as #devin-runs) for coordinating Devin sessions. The standard workflow involves a team member creating a GitHub issue, assigning it to Devin (or tagging @Devin in Slack), after which Devin writes code, runs tests, and opens a PR for the team to review.[&#91;23&#93;](#cite_note-23)

### Playbooks

Playbooks are a feature allowing teams to create reusable documents that provide Devin with the steps and context needed to complete common engineering tasks. Unlike rigid programs, Playbooks use natural language to specify not only the steps Devin needs to follow but also success criteria and guardrails. Once one engineer successfully coaches Devin to complete a task, others on the team can replicate that success using the same Playbook.[&#91;23&#93;](#cite_note-23)

### Enterprise features

Enterprise deployments of Devin include additional security and control capabilities:

- Virtual Private Cloud (VPC) deployment options
- Single sign-on (SSO) authentication
- Admin dashboards for monitoring performance
- Custom security policies and access controls[&#91;23&#93;](#cite_note-23)

## SWE-bench performance

### Initial benchmark results

At launch, Cognition reported that Devin resolved 13.86% of issues on [SWE-bench](/wiki/swe-bench) Lite end-to-end without human assistance, resolving 79 out of 570 issues. This score significantly exceeded the previous state-of-the-art: Claude 2 achieved 4.80% in the "assisted" setting (where the model was given the correct files to edit) and 1.96% in the "unassisted" setting. Devin's evaluation was conducted in the unassisted, agent setting, making the comparison especially notable.[&#91;24&#93;](#cite_note-24)[&#91;25&#93;](#cite_note-25)

### Methodology

Cognition published a technical report detailing the evaluation methodology:

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Benchmark variant | SWE-bench Lite (a subset of the full SWE-bench) |
| Test set size | 570 issues (a randomly chosen 25% of the 2,294-issue test set) |
| Input | Only the GitHub issue description (no file lists, no hints) |
| Runtime limit | 45 minutes per issue |
| Setting | Fully autonomous (no human input during runs) |
| Evaluation | After each run, test files are reset to the original state; the agent's patch is applied, followed by the test patch; evaluation commands check whether all tests pass |

The decision to use a 25% subset was made to reduce evaluation time, following the same strategy used by the SWE-bench authors in their original paper. Unlike previous LLM evaluations, where models received lists of relevant files ("assisted" mode) or had files selected via retrieval systems, Devin navigated the file system entirely on its own.[&#91;24&#93;](#cite_note-24)

### Controversy around benchmark claims

Several aspects of the SWE-bench claims drew criticism from the community:

- **Benchmark variant confusion**: Cognition's marketing materials sometimes presented the 13.86% score without clearly distinguishing between SWE-bench Lite, Full, and [SWE-bench Verified](/wiki/swe-bench_verified) (a human-validated subset of 500 problems released by [OpenAI](/wiki/openai) in August 2024). The denominator and difficulty differ significantly across these variants, making comparisons misleading if the variant is not specified.[&#91;26&#93;](#cite_note-26)
- **Subset evaluation**: The fact that Devin was evaluated on only 25% of the test set, rather than the full benchmark, raised questions about whether the results were representative.
- **Rapid obsolescence**: Within months of Devin's announcement, other systems surpassed its SWE-bench scores. By late 2025, leading models achieved scores exceeding 70% on SWE-bench Verified.[&#91;27&#93;](#cite_note-27)

## Product versions and pricing

### Early access (March to December 2024)

Following the March 2024 announcement, Devin was available only to a limited number of users through an early access program. During this period, the product underwent significant iteration based on user feedback.[&#91;28&#93;](#cite_note-28)

### General availability: Devin 1.x (December 2024)

On December 10, 2024, Cognition made Devin generally available at a starting price of $500 per month for engineering teams. The GA launch included no seat limits and access to Devin's Slack integration, IDE extension, and API, along with onboarding sessions and support from the Cognition engineering team. As part of the launch, Cognition also announced the Devin Open Source Initiative, providing selected open source maintainers with 500 free Agent Compute Units (ACUs) on a Devin Teams plan.[&#91;28&#93;](#cite_note-28)

### Devin 2.0 (April 2025)

In April 2025, Cognition released Devin 2.0, representing a complete overhaul that addressed both capability limitations and accessibility barriers from version 1.x. Key improvements included:[&#91;29&#93;](#cite_note-29)[&#91;30&#93;](#cite_note-30)

- **Agent-Native IDE**: A new interactive, cloud-based IDE experience allowing users to spin up multiple parallel Devin sessions, each with its own workspace.
- **Devin Search and Wiki**: Enhanced codebase understanding capabilities for navigating unfamiliar codebases, recognizing architectural patterns, and documenting findings.
- **Interactive planning**: Devin can analyze an entire codebase, identify relevant files, and map out a detailed, step-by-step plan before writing any code.
- **Dynamic re-planning**: If Devin encounters a roadblock, it can alter its strategy without requiring human intervention.
- **Performance improvements**: Devin 2.0 completes 83% more junior-level development tasks per Agent Compute Unit (ACU) compared to version 1.x, through improved reasoning, better error recovery, and smarter resource allocation.

### Current pricing (as of 2025)

Devin 2.0 dramatically reduced the entry barrier from $500 per month to $20 per month for the Core plan.

| Plan | Monthly Price | ACUs Included | Additional ACU Cost | Target Users |
|------|--------------|---------------|---------------------|--------------|

| Team | $500 | 250 ACUs | $2.00 per ACU | Medium-sized engineering teams |
| Enterprise | Custom | Custom | Custom | Large organizations requiring VPC, SSO, and admin controls |

Agent Compute Units (ACUs) are Cognition's billing unit, representing a measure of the computational resources consumed during a Devin session. The cost scales with the duration and complexity of the task being performed.[&#91;30&#93;](#cite_note-30)[&#91;31&#93;](#cite_note-31)

## Technical architecture

### System design

Devin is a cloud-hosted agent that runs entirely in the cloud, never touching the user's local machine. Each session creates a fresh sandboxed environment containing a full Linux computing environment with shell access, a code editor, and a headless web browser. The architecture is designed around a modular pipeline:[&#91;2&#93;](#cite_note-2)[&#91;22&#93;](#cite_note-22)

1. **Task intake**: The user provides a natural language description of the desired work, either via Slack, the web UI, the VS Code extension, or the API.
2. **Planning**: Devin's Planner module analyzes the request, examines the codebase, and generates a step-by-step execution plan.
3. **Execution**: The agent carries out the plan by writing and editing code, running shell commands, browsing the web for documentation, installing dependencies, and running tests.
4. **Iteration**: If tests fail or errors arise, Devin autonomously debugs and iterates until the build succeeds or the runtime limit is reached.
5. **Delivery**: The completed work is submitted as a pull request, and the user is notified via Slack or the web UI.

### Reasoning engine

Cognition has described Devin's core differentiator as advances in long-term reasoning and planning. Unlike code-completion systems that operate on a line-by-line or function-by-function basis, Devin is designed to maintain a coherent plan across thousands of individual decisions spanning the lifecycle of a complex engineering task. The system can recall relevant context from earlier in a session, identify when its approach is not working, and pivot to alternative strategies.[&#91;1&#93;](#cite_note-1)

### Cognition's proprietary models

While Cognition has not publicly disclosed full details of Devin's underlying model architecture, the company has stated that it develops proprietary AI models focused specifically on software engineering reasoning. The system is designed to combine [large language model](/wiki/large_language_model) capabilities with agent-specific infrastructure for tool use, memory management, and multi-step planning.[&#91;6&#93;](#cite_note-6)

## Real-world performance

### Independent evaluations

Several independent assessments have provided a more nuanced picture of Devin's real-world capabilities compared to its benchmark scores and marketing materials.

#### Answer.AI review (January 2025)

In January 2025, three data scientists affiliated with Answer.AI (an AI research lab founded by Jeremy Howard and Eric Ries) published a detailed account of using Devin over the course of a month. Out of 20 tasks attempted, only 3 were completed successfully, with 14 failures and 3 inconclusive results.[&#91;32&#93;](#cite_note-32)

Key findings from the review included:

- **Unpredictable performance**: The team found they were unable to predict which tasks would succeed. Tasks similar to early wins sometimes failed in complex, time-consuming ways.
- **Early wins on defined tasks**: For well-scoped tasks (such as pulling data from a Notion database into Google Sheets), Devin demonstrated competence, navigating API documentation, handling credential setup, and producing a working solution in about an hour with only minutes of human interaction.
- **Autonomous overreach**: Devin's autonomous nature became a liability in some cases. It would spend days pursuing impossible solutions rather than recognizing fundamental blockers and asking for help.
- **Tooling difficulties**: Devin struggled to use internal tooling critical to the team's workflow, even when provided with extensive documentation and examples.
- **Dangerous outputs**: In one instance, Devin generated a migration script that would have deleted data from a source table rather than consolidating it, a critical error that would have caused data loss in production.[&#91;32&#93;](#cite_note-32)[&#91;33&#93;](#cite_note-33)

#### The Register report (January 2025)

The Register published a report noting that Devin "fails 85% of its assigned tasks" based on aggregated independent testing data, with a real-world complex task completion rate of approximately 14 to 15 percent.[&#91;33&#93;](#cite_note-33)

#### Other assessments

Additional independent reviews have noted that Devin excels at well-defined tasks that would typically take a junior engineer 4 to 8 hours (bug fixes, test writing, dependency upgrades, code migrations, and CRUD feature implementation) but struggles with ambiguous requirements, complex architectural decisions, and open-ended design work. Engineers have anecdotally reported defect rates in agent-generated code at roughly 1.5 to 2 times higher than senior-developer-authored code for equivalent task complexity.[&#91;21&#93;](#cite_note-21)

### Cognition's own performance data (2025)

In its 2025 annual performance review blog post, Cognition acknowledged areas for improvement while highlighting progress. According to the company's own metrics, Devin had become "4x faster at problem solving and 2x more efficient in resource consumption," and the PR merge rate had improved from 34% to 67% year over year. The company also reported that customers achieved 6 to 12 times efficiency gains on well-scoped tasks.[&#91;34&#93;](#cite_note-34)[&#91;21&#93;](#cite_note-21)

### Notable customer use cases

| Customer | Use Case | Reported Results |
|----------|----------|-----------------|
| Nubank | Migrating over 6 million lines of code into smaller modules (originally expected to require 1,000+ engineers for 18 months) | 8-12x improvement in engineering efficiency, 20x cost savings |
| Goldman Sachs | Deployed as autonomous full-stack developer for internal engineering workflows | First major bank to use Devin |
| Cognition (internal) | Uses Devin to build Devin itself | Undisclosed internal efficiency metrics |

The typical reported use cases where Devin performs best include clearing backlogs of routine tasks (bug fixes, test coverage, dependency upgrades), code migrations (handled 10 to 14 times faster than manual effort), security fixes (accomplished in an average of 1.5 minutes versus 30 minutes for a human developer), and test generation (boosting coverage from 50-60% to 80-90%).[&#91;21&#93;](#cite_note-21)[&#91;15&#93;](#cite_note-15)

### Philosophical differences

The market has consolidated around three distinct philosophies for AI-assisted coding:[&#91;35&#93;](#cite_note-35)

1. **The Ecosystem Play** ([GitHub Copilot](/wiki/github_copilot)): Leverages the GitHub ecosystem, integrating AI suggestions directly into existing developer workflows with per-seat licensing.
2. **The Native Editor** (Cursor, Windsurf): Rebuilds the IDE from the ground up around AI, indexing entire codebases for deep contextual understanding.
3. **The Autonomous Employee** (Devin): Operates as a virtual team member that independently plans, executes, and delivers complete engineering tasks with compute-based billing.

## Criticism and skepticism

### Marketing versus reality

Devin's positioning as "the first AI software engineer" has been one of the most criticized aspects of the product. Critics argue that the marketing language implies a level of autonomy and reliability that the product does not consistently deliver. The Upwork demonstration debunking by Internet of Bugs, the disparity between SWE-bench scores and real-world performance, and the approximately 15% success rate on complex tasks reported by independent testers have all contributed to skepticism about Cognition's claims.[&#91;18&#93;](#cite_note-18)[&#91;32&#93;](#cite_note-32)[&#91;33&#93;](#cite_note-33)

### Job displacement concerns

The announcement of Devin occurred during a period of significant layoffs in the technology industry. Many software engineers expressed concern that tools like Devin could be used by employers to justify further reductions in engineering headcount, particularly at the junior level. Others countered that Devin is better understood as a productivity tool that handles routine tasks, freeing senior engineers to focus on higher-level design and architecture work.[&#91;17&#93;](#cite_note-17)

### Quality and safety concerns

Independent reviewers have identified several categories of concern regarding Devin's outputs:

- **Dangerous code generation**: In multiple reported instances, Devin generated code that would have caused data loss, security vulnerabilities, or other production issues if deployed without careful human review.[&#91;32&#93;](#cite_note-32)
- **Over-complexity**: Devin sometimes generates overly complex solutions for straightforward problems, producing verbose or convoluted code where a simpler approach would suffice.[&#91;32&#93;](#cite_note-32)
- **Lack of uncertainty signaling**: Devin does not always surface its own uncertainty or flag potentially dangerous actions, which can lead to costly mistakes if outputs are not carefully reviewed.[&#91;32&#93;](#cite_note-32)
- **Higher defect rates**: Agent-generated code has been anecdotally reported as having defect rates roughly 1.5 to 2 times higher than code written by senior developers for equivalent tasks.[&#91;21&#93;](#cite_note-21)

### Benchmark limitations

The broader AI community has raised concerns about the use of SWE-bench as a measure of real-world software engineering ability. Critics note that the benchmark evaluates performance on specific types of GitHub issues (primarily bug fixes in well-known open-source Python repositories) and does not capture the full breadth of software engineering work, including system design, requirements gathering, team collaboration, code review, and maintenance of large-scale production systems.[&#91;26&#93;](#cite_note-26)

### Valuation skepticism

Some observers have questioned whether Cognition's $10.2 billion valuation (as of September 2025) is justified given the product's real-world performance limitations and the rapidly intensifying competition from well-funded players like GitHub, OpenAI, and Anthropic. The company's aggressive growth in ARR (from $1M to $73M in nine months) has partially assuaged these concerns, though critics note that much of the growth may be driven by hype rather than sustained enterprise adoption.[&#91;5&#93;](#cite_note-5)[&#91;36&#93;](#cite_note-36)
