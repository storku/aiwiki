# EU AI Act

The **EU AI Act** (formally **Regulation (EU) 2024/1689**) is the world's first comprehensive legal framework regulating [artificial intelligence](/wiki/artificial_intelligence). Adopted by the European Union, the regulation establishes a harmonized set of rules for the development, placement on the market, putting into service, and use of AI systems across all 27 EU Member States. The Act entered into force on 1 August 2024 and is being implemented in phases, with full applicability by 2 August 2027.

The regulation follows a **risk-based approach**, classifying AI systems into four tiers: unacceptable risk (prohibited), high risk, limited risk, and minimal risk. It imposes strict obligations on providers and deployers of AI systems proportional to the level of risk their systems pose, with the goal of ensuring that AI deployed in Europe is safe, transparent, traceable, non-discriminatory, and environmentally sustainable, while maintaining human oversight.

The EU AI Act is widely considered a landmark piece of legislation with global implications. Much like the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) before it, the Act has extraterritorial reach, applying not only to organizations within the EU but also to providers and deployers located outside the EU whose AI systems produce outputs used within the Union. This regulatory approach has been described as an example of the "Brussels Effect," whereby EU standards effectively become global norms because multinational companies find it more efficient to adopt a single compliance framework worldwide.

## Legislative History

The EU AI Act underwent a complex and lengthy legislative process spanning more than three years, from initial proposal to final enactment.

### Background and White Paper (2020)

In February 2020, the European Commission published a **White Paper on Artificial Intelligence**, outlining policy options for a future regulatory framework for AI. The White Paper set out the Commission's vision for AI regulation centered on the concepts of "excellence" and "trust," and launched a public consultation that received over 1,200 responses from stakeholders including governments, industry, academia, and civil society organizations.

### Commission Proposal (April 2021)

On **21 April 2021**, the European Commission officially proposed the Artificial Intelligence Act as part of its broader digital strategy. The proposal was presented by the Commission under the leadership of Executive Vice-President Margrethe Vestager and Internal Market Commissioner Thierry Breton. It was the first-ever legislative proposal by a major jurisdiction to establish a horizontal regulatory framework for AI. The public consultation period ended on 6 August 2021.

### Council and Parliament Deliberation (2022)

On **25 January 2022**, the lead committees of the European Parliament, the Committee on the Internal Market and Consumer Protection (IMCO) and the Committee on Civil Liberties, Justice and Home Affairs (LIBE), held their first joint exchange of views on the AI Act proposal. The two committees jointly led negotiations on the Parliament's side.

On **6 December 2022**, the Council of the European Union adopted its **general approach** (common negotiating position), enabling trilogue negotiations with the European Parliament to begin.

### Parliamentary Approval and Trilogue (2023)

The European Parliament adopted its negotiating position on **14 June 2023**, with strong support across political groups. The emergence of [generative AI](/wiki/generative_model) systems such as [ChatGPT](/wiki/chatgpt) during the legislative process prompted lawmakers to add provisions specifically addressing [foundation models](/wiki/foundation_model) and general-purpose AI (GPAI), which were not part of the original 2021 proposal.

Trilogue negotiations between the European Parliament, the Council, and the Commission commenced in earnest in the summer of 2023. On **9 December 2023**, after three days of intensive "marathon" negotiations, the EU Council and Parliament reached a **provisional political agreement** on the final text of the AI Act.

### Final Adoption and Entry into Force (2024)

On **2 February 2024**, the Committee of Permanent Representatives (Coreper) unanimously endorsed the compromise text.

On **13 March 2024**, the European Parliament adopted the final text in a plenary vote, with **523 votes in favor, 46 against, and 49 abstentions**.

The Council of the European Union formally approved the regulation on **21 May 2024**.

The AI Act was published in the **Official Journal of the European Union on 12 July 2024** and entered into force on **1 August 2024**, twenty days after publication, in accordance with standard EU legislative procedure.

## Scope and Territorial Application

### Who Is Covered

The AI Act applies broadly to multiple actors in the AI value chain, including:

- **Providers** (developers) of AI systems or GPAI models that place them on the market or put them into service in the EU, regardless of whether the provider is established within the EU or in a third country.
- **Deployers** (users) of AI systems that are located within the EU.
- **Importers** and **distributors** of AI systems within the EU market.
- **Product manufacturers** who place on the market or put into service an AI system together with their product under their own name or trademark.

### Extraterritorial Reach

A distinctive and significant feature of the AI Act is its **extraterritorial scope**. Under Article 2(1)(c), the regulation applies to providers and deployers of AI systems that are established or located in a third country, where the **output produced by the AI system is used in the EU**. This "output-based" jurisdictional hook is designed to prevent companies from circumventing the rules by processing AI outside Europe and then transmitting results into the Union.

Non-EU providers placing AI systems on the EU market must appoint an **authorized representative** established within the EU (as defined under Article 3(5)), who accepts a written mandate to carry out obligations and procedures on their behalf.

### Exemptions and Exclusions

The AI Act does **not** apply to:

- **Military, defense, and national security purposes**: AI systems used for these purposes are excluded regardless of whether the entity carrying out the activity is public or private. However, if such a system is later repurposed for non-military use, it falls back within scope.
- **Scientific research and development**: AI systems developed and used solely for scientific research purposes, outside of commercial activity, are generally exempt.
- **Personal, non-professional use**: Individuals using AI systems for purely personal, non-professional activities are not subject to the regulation.
- **AI systems released under free and open-source licenses** benefit from certain (but not all) exemptions, as described in the GPAI section below.
- **Third-country public authorities**: AI systems used by public authorities or international organizations in third countries, under international cooperation or law enforcement agreements with the EU, are exempt provided adequate safeguards for fundamental rights exist.

## Risk-Based Classification System

The cornerstone of the EU AI Act is its **risk-based classification framework**, which categorizes AI systems into four tiers according to the level of risk they pose to health, safety, and fundamental rights of persons. Obligations increase proportionally with the level of risk.

### Overview of Risk Tiers

| Risk Level | Description | Regulatory Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Unacceptable Risk** | AI practices that pose a clear threat to fundamental rights and EU values | **Prohibited entirely** |
| **High Risk** | AI systems with significant potential to harm health, safety, or fundamental rights | Subject to strict requirements before market entry and throughout lifecycle |
| **Limited Risk** | AI systems that interact directly with people or generate content | Subject to transparency obligations |
| **Minimal / No Risk** | The vast majority of AI systems currently in use | No specific obligations (voluntary codes of conduct encouraged) |

## Prohibited AI Practices (Unacceptable Risk)

Article 5 of the AI Act identifies **eight categories of AI practices** deemed to pose unacceptable risks and are therefore **banned outright** across the EU. These prohibitions took legal effect on **2 February 2025**, making them the first provisions of the Act to become enforceable.

### 1. Subliminal Manipulation

AI systems that deploy **subliminal techniques** beyond a person's consciousness, or that use purposefully **manipulative or deceptive techniques**, with the objective or effect of materially distorting a person's behavior in a manner that causes or is reasonably likely to cause that person or another person significant harm.

### 2. Exploitation of Vulnerabilities

AI systems that exploit **vulnerabilities of specific groups** of persons due to their age, disability, or a specific social or economic situation, with the objective or effect of materially distorting the behavior of a person belonging to that group in a manner that causes or is reasonably likely to cause significant harm.

### 3. Social Scoring

AI systems used by public authorities or on their behalf for the **evaluation or classification of natural persons** or groups over a certain period of time based on their social behavior or known, inferred, or predicted personal or personality characteristics, resulting in detrimental or unfavorable treatment that is unjustified or disproportionate to their social behavior or its gravity.

### 4. Individual Criminal Risk Assessment Based Solely on Profiling

AI systems that assess or predict the risk of a natural person committing a criminal offense **based solely on profiling** or on an assessment of personality traits and characteristics. This prohibition does not apply to AI systems used to support human assessment of the involvement of a person in criminal activity based on objective, verifiable facts directly linked to criminal activity.

### 5. Untargeted Scraping for Facial Recognition Databases

AI systems that create or expand **facial recognition databases** through the **untargeted scraping** of facial images from the internet or from closed-circuit television (CCTV) footage.

### 6. Emotion Recognition in Workplaces and Education

AI systems used to infer the **emotions of natural persons** in the areas of the **workplace** and **education institutions**, except where the AI system is intended to be put into service or placed on the market for medical or safety reasons (for example, monitoring the fatigue levels of a pilot).

### 7. Biometric Categorization Using Sensitive Attributes

**Biometric categorization systems** that categorize individual natural persons based on their biometric data to deduce or infer their **race, political opinions, trade union membership, religious or philosophical beliefs, sex life, or sexual orientation**. This prohibition does not cover labeling or filtering of lawfully acquired biometric datasets (such as images) based on biometric data, or categorization of biometric data in the area of law enforcement.

### 8. Real-Time Remote Biometric Identification in Public Spaces

The use of **real-time remote biometric identification systems** (such as live facial recognition) in publicly accessible spaces for the purposes of law enforcement, subject to narrow exceptions. The exceptions allow use only when **strictly necessary** for:

- The targeted search for specific victims of abduction, trafficking, or sexual exploitation, and the search for missing persons.
- The prevention of a specific, substantial, and imminent threat to the life or physical safety of natural persons, or a genuine and present or foreseeable threat of a terrorist attack.
- The localization or identification of a suspect of a criminal offense punishable in the Member State concerned by a custodial sentence of at least four years.

Even under these exceptions, the use of real-time biometric identification requires **prior judicial authorization** (or, in duly justified urgent situations, authorization may be sought during or after use, with a requirement to request it within 24 hours).

## High-Risk AI Systems

### Classification Criteria

AI systems are classified as **high-risk** under one of two pathways defined in Article 6:

**Pathway 1 (Article 6(1), Annex I):** The AI system is intended to be used as a **safety component** of a product, or is itself a product, covered by the EU harmonization legislation listed in **Annex I** of the AI Act. This annex references more than 30 EU directives and regulations governing product safety, including legislation on:

- Medical devices and in vitro diagnostic medical devices
- Civil aviation safety
- Motor vehicles and their trailers
- Agricultural and forestry vehicles
- Marine equipment
- Rail system interoperability
- Machinery and lifts
- Equipment for use in potentially explosive atmospheres
- Radio equipment
- Pressure equipment
- Cableway installations
- Personal protective equipment
- Toys
- Recreational craft

For this pathway, the AI system must also require a **third-party conformity assessment** under the relevant sectoral legislation.

**Pathway 2 (Article 6(2), Annex III):** The AI system falls within one of the **eight high-risk use case areas** listed in **Annex III** of the regulation.

### Annex III: High-Risk Use Case Areas

| Area | Examples of High-Risk AI Systems |
|---|---|
| **1. Biometrics** | Remote biometric identification (not in real-time), biometric categorization by protected attributes, emotion recognition systems |
| **2. Critical Infrastructure** | AI used as safety components in management and operation of critical digital infrastructure, road traffic, and supply of water, gas, heating, or electricity |
| **3. Education and Vocational Training** | AI determining access to or admission into educational institutions; AI evaluating learning outcomes, including systems used to assess students in examinations |
| **4. Employment, Workers Management, and Access to Self-Employment** | AI used in recruitment, screening, or filtering of applications; AI for decisions on promotion, termination, task allocation, or monitoring and evaluating performance and behavior |
| **5. Access to Essential Private and Public Services** | AI evaluating eligibility for public assistance benefits; AI evaluating creditworthiness or establishing credit scores; AI used in risk assessment and pricing for life and health insurance; AI used to dispatch or prioritize emergency first response services |
| **6. Law Enforcement** | AI for individual risk assessment of criminal offending or reoffending; AI used as polygraphs; AI for evaluating reliability of evidence; AI for profiling during detection, investigation, or prosecution |
| **7. Migration, Asylum, and Border Control Management** | AI used as polygraphs or for inferring emotional states; AI for assessing security, irregular migration, or health risks; AI for verifying authenticity of travel documents; AI for examining applications for asylum, visa, and residence permits |
| **8. Administration of Justice and Democratic Processes** | AI assisting judicial authorities in researching and interpreting facts and law; AI used for influencing the outcome of elections or referendums (not including AI used for organizational or logistical aspects of campaigns) |

A notable qualification exists in Article 6(3): an AI system listed in Annex III is **not** considered high-risk if it does not pose a **significant risk of harm** to the health, safety, or fundamental rights of natural persons, including by not materially influencing the outcome of decision-making. However, a system is always deemed high-risk if it performs **profiling of natural persons**.

### Requirements for High-Risk AI Systems

Providers of high-risk AI systems must comply with an extensive set of requirements spanning the entire lifecycle of the system. These are specified in Chapter III, Section 2 (Articles 8 through 15) of the Act.

#### Risk Management System (Article 9)

Providers must establish, implement, document, and maintain a **risk management system** as a continuous iterative process planned and run throughout the entire lifecycle of the high-risk AI system. This system must:

- Identify and analyze known and reasonably foreseeable risks to health, safety, or fundamental rights.
- Estimate and evaluate risks that may emerge when the system is used in accordance with its intended purpose and under conditions of reasonably foreseeable misuse.
- Adopt appropriate and targeted risk management measures to address identified risks.
- Specifically consider whether the high-risk AI system may adversely affect persons under the age of 18 or other vulnerable groups.
- Ensure residual risks are communicated to deployers.

#### Data and Data Governance (Article 10)

High-risk AI systems that involve the training of models with data must be developed on the basis of **training, validation, and testing datasets** that meet specific quality criteria. Data governance must:

- Address relevant design choices.
- Include processes for data collection, data preparation, and data labeling.
- Examine data for possible [biases](/wiki/bias) that could lead to discrimination or fundamental rights violations.
- Identify data gaps or shortcomings and appropriate measures to address them.
- Consider characteristics specific to the geographic, contextual, behavioral, or functional setting in which the system is intended to be used.

#### Technical Documentation (Article 11)

Providers must draw up technical documentation before the system is placed on the market or put into service. The documentation must contain all information necessary to demonstrate that the system complies with the requirements and provide national competent authorities and notified bodies with the information needed to assess compliance.

#### Record-Keeping and Logging (Article 12)

High-risk AI systems must be designed and developed with capabilities enabling the **automatic recording of events (logs)** over the lifetime of the system. Logging capabilities must ensure a level of traceability appropriate to the intended purpose of the system.

#### Transparency and Provision of Information to Deployers (Article 13)

High-risk AI systems must be designed and developed to ensure that their operation is sufficiently **transparent** to enable deployers to interpret the system's output and use it appropriately. Instructions for use must accompany the system, including information about the provider, system characteristics, capabilities, limitations, human oversight measures, expected lifetime, and maintenance requirements.

#### Human Oversight (Article 14)

High-risk AI systems must be designed and developed to enable effective **human oversight** during the period in which they are in use. Human oversight measures must:

- Be identified and built into the system by the provider, or identified as appropriate to be implemented by the deployer.
- Enable the individuals assigned to human oversight to properly understand the relevant capabilities and limitations of the system.
- Enable those individuals to correctly interpret the system's output.
- Enable those individuals to decide not to use the system, to override or reverse the output, or to intervene in or interrupt the operation of the system.

#### Accuracy, Robustness, and Cybersecurity (Article 15)

High-risk AI systems must be designed and developed to achieve an appropriate level of **accuracy**, **robustness**, and **cybersecurity**, and to perform consistently in those respects throughout their lifecycle. Accuracy levels and relevant metrics must be declared in accompanying instructions of use.

### Obligations of Providers and Deployers

**Providers** of high-risk AI systems have extensive obligations, including:

- Ensuring their AI systems comply with all requirements before market placement.
- Implementing a **quality management system**.
- Retaining documentation and logs.
- Undergoing **conformity assessment** procedures before placing the system on the market.
- Registering the system in the EU database for high-risk AI systems.
- Taking corrective actions when the system is not in conformity and informing distributors, deployers, and authorities.
- Affixing the **CE marking** to their systems to indicate conformity.
- Cooperating with competent authorities upon request.

**Deployers** of high-risk AI systems must:

- Use the systems in accordance with the instructions of use.
- Assign human oversight to competent and trained natural persons.
- Monitor the operation of the system and inform the provider or distributor of any risks.
- Retain logs automatically generated by the system (for at least six months, unless otherwise required).
- Carry out a **fundamental rights impact assessment** before putting the system into use (for public bodies and certain private entities).

### Right to Explanation (Article 86)

Any person subject to a decision taken by a deployer on the basis of output from a high-risk AI system that produces legal effects or similarly significantly affects that person has the **right to obtain clear and meaningful explanations** of the role of the AI system in the decision-making procedure and the main elements of the decision taken.

### Right to Lodge Complaints (Article 85)

Natural persons and legal entities have the right to lodge complaints with the relevant **market surveillance authority** regarding non-compliance with the regulation.

## General-Purpose AI (GPAI) Model Obligations

The provisions on **General-Purpose AI models** were introduced during the legislative process in response to the rapid emergence of [large language models](/wiki/large_language_model) and other [foundation models](/wiki/foundation_model) such as [GPT-4](/wiki/gpt-4), [Claude](/wiki/claude_ai), [Gemini](/wiki/gemini), and [LLaMA](/wiki/llama). These provisions are found in Chapter V of the Act (Articles 51 through 56).

### Definition of GPAI Models

A general-purpose AI model is defined as an AI model, including when trained with a large amount of data using self-supervision at scale, that displays significant generality and is capable of competently performing a wide range of distinct tasks regardless of the way the model is placed on the market, and which can be integrated into a variety of downstream systems or applications. This definition is technology-neutral and intended to capture models like [large language models](/wiki/large_language_model) and multimodal models.

A model trained using more than **10^23 FLOPs** (floating point operations) is generally presumed to qualify as a GPAI model, although this is not an absolute threshold: a model exceeding that compute level but limited to narrow tasks may not qualify, while a model below that threshold may still qualify if it demonstrates sufficient generality.

### Obligations for All GPAI Model Providers (Article 53)

All providers of GPAI models must:

| Obligation | Description |
|---|---|
| **Technical documentation** | Draw up and keep up to date technical documentation of the model, including its training and testing process and the results of its evaluation, and make it available to the AI Office and national competent authorities upon request |
| **Information to downstream providers** | Draw up and keep up to date information and documentation for providers of AI systems who intend to integrate the GPAI model into their systems |
| **Copyright compliance** | Put in place a policy to comply with Union copyright law, in particular to identify and comply with reservations of rights expressed by rights holders under Article 4(3) of Directive (EU) 2019/790 |
| **Training data summary** | Draw up and make publicly available a sufficiently detailed summary about the content used for training of the GPAI model, according to a template provided by the AI Office |

### GPAI Models with Systemic Risk (Articles 51 and 55)

A GPAI model is classified as posing **systemic risk** if it has **high-impact capabilities**. A model is presumed to have high-impact capabilities when the cumulative amount of computation used for its training measured in FLOPs is greater than **10^25**. The Commission may also designate a model as having systemic risk based on other criteria, including the number of registered end users, the degree of autonomy of the model, or the model's impact on the internal market.

Well-known models classified (or presumed to be classified) as systemic-risk GPAI include GPT-4, Gemini 1.5 Pro, Grok 3, and Claude 3.7 Sonnet, among others.

Providers of GPAI models with systemic risk must notify the Commission within **two weeks** of reasonably foreseeing or actually reaching the 10^25 FLOPs threshold.

In addition to the general GPAI obligations, providers of systemic-risk GPAI models must:

- Perform **model evaluations**, including conducting and documenting **adversarial testing** (red-teaming), to identify and mitigate systemic risks, using standardized protocols and tools.
- Assess and mitigate possible **systemic risks**, including their sources, which may arise from development, market placement, or use.
- Track, document, and **report serious incidents** and possible corrective measures to the AI Office and relevant national competent authorities without undue delay.
- Ensure an adequate level of **cybersecurity protection** for the model and its physical infrastructure.

### Open-Source GPAI Exemptions

The AI Act provides **limited exemptions** for GPAI models released under free and open-source licenses. Open-source GPAI providers are exempt from the obligations to provide detailed technical documentation to downstream providers and to the AI Office. However, they must still:

- Publish a sufficiently detailed summary of training data.
- Establish a copyright compliance policy.

Critically, **open-source GPAI models with systemic risk receive no exemptions** and must comply with all obligations applicable to systemic-risk models, including adversarial testing, incident reporting, and cybersecurity requirements.

### Code of Practice for GPAI Models

The European Commission published the **General-Purpose AI Code of Practice** on 10 July 2025 as a voluntary tool to help industry comply with the AI Act's GPAI rules, which became applicable on 2 August 2025. The Code was developed through a multi-stakeholder process involving industry, civil society, and academia.

As of 2025, **26 organizations** have signed the full Code, including Amazon, [Anthropic](/wiki/anthropic), Google, IBM, Microsoft, and [OpenAI](/wiki/openai), alongside firms like Aleph Alpha, Cohere, and [Mistral AI](/wiki/mistral_ai). Notably, [Meta](/wiki/meta_ai_company) has refused to sign, with its Chief Global Affairs Officer characterizing the Code as "overreach" and warning that the requirements would stifle AI innovation in Europe.

### Provider Status and Fine-Tuning

A downstream actor who modifies an existing GPAI model becomes the **new provider** of that model if the training compute used for the modification exceeds **one-third** of the compute used to train the original model. Below that threshold, the downstream actor is generally not considered a new provider.

## Transparency Requirements

The AI Act establishes specific **transparency obligations** for certain categories of AI systems under Article 50, primarily targeting systems classified as "limited risk." These requirements are designed to ensure that people are aware when they are interacting with AI or consuming AI-generated content.

### AI Systems Interacting Directly with Persons

Providers must ensure that AI systems intended for **direct interaction with natural persons** (such as [chatbots](/wiki/chatgpt)) are designed and developed so that the natural person is informed that they are interacting with an AI system, unless this is **obvious from the circumstances and context of use** to a reasonably well-informed, observant, and cautious person. This requirement does not apply to AI systems authorized by law for the detection, prevention, investigation, or prosecution of criminal offenses.

### AI-Generated or Manipulated Content

Providers of AI systems that **generate synthetic audio, image, video, or text content** must ensure that the outputs are:

- **Marked in a machine-readable format** and detectable as artificially generated or manipulated.
- Implemented through **technical solutions** that are effective, interoperable, robust, and reliable, as far as is technically feasible.

### Deepfakes

Deployers of AI systems that generate or manipulate image, audio, or video content constituting a **deep fake** must disclose that the content has been artificially generated or manipulated. An exception applies where the content forms part of an evidently **artistic, creative, satirical, fictional, or analogous work or program**; in such cases, the transparency obligation is limited to disclosure of the existence of such generated or manipulated content in a manner that does not hamper the display or enjoyment of the work.

### Emotion Recognition and Biometric Categorization

Deployers of **emotion recognition systems** or **biometric categorization systems** must inform the natural persons exposed to them of the operation of the system and process personal data in accordance with applicable EU law, including the GDPR.

### AI-Generated Text on Public Interest Matters

When AI systems generate or manipulate **text that is published for the purpose of informing the public on matters of public interest**, the deployer must disclose that the content has been artificially generated or manipulated, unless the content has undergone a process of human review or editorial control and a natural or legal person holds editorial responsibility for the publication.

## Governance and Enforcement

The EU AI Act establishes a multi-layered governance and enforcement structure involving both EU-level and national bodies.

### EU AI Office

The **European AI Office** was established by Commission Decision C(2024) 390 final on 24 January 2024 (entering into force on 21 February 2024) within the European Commission's Directorate-General for Communications Networks, Content and Technology (DG CNECT). It serves as the **center of AI expertise** and forms the foundation of the EU's AI governance system.

The AI Office is organized into multiple units covering:

- Excellence in AI and Robotics
- Regulation and Compliance
- AI Safety
- AI Innovation and Policy Coordination
- AI for Societal Good

It is supported by a Lead Scientific Advisor and an Advisor for International Affairs. The Office employs more than **125 staff** (as of 2025), including technology specialists, lawyers, policy specialists, economists, and administrative staff, with plans to grow to approximately 140 employees.

The AI Office holds **exclusive jurisdiction** over enforcement of the Act's provisions relating to GPAI models. This means that, unlike enforcement for other AI systems (which is handled by national authorities), the Commission, through the AI Office, has sole supervisory and enforcement powers over GPAI model providers.

### European Artificial Intelligence Board

The **European AI Board** consists of one representative from each of the 27 EU Member States, appointed for three-year terms, with one member serving as chair. The AI Office and the European Data Protection Supervisor participate as observers without voting powers.

The Board's primary functions include:

- Advising and assisting Member States and the Commission to ensure consistent and effective application of the AI Act across the EU.
- Facilitating the exchange of best practices among Member States.
- Contributing to uniform administrative practices in the Member States.
- Issuing opinions, recommendations, and written contributions on matters related to the implementation of the AI Act.

### Scientific Panel of Independent Experts

The **Scientific Panel** is composed of independent experts in the field of AI, selected for their scientific or technical expertise. Its role is to:

- Provide technical advice and input to the AI Office and the Board.
- Support the AI Office's enforcement activities, particularly regarding GPAI models with systemic risk.
- Contribute to the development of methodologies for evaluating capabilities of GPAI models.
- Advise on the classification and designation of GPAI models with systemic risk.
- Support the monitoring of potential risks from frontier AI development.

### Advisory Forum

The **Advisory Forum** represents a balanced selection of **stakeholders**, including industry representatives, startups and SMEs, academia, think tanks, and civil society. It provides input to the AI Board and the Commission and serves as a channel for diverse stakeholder perspectives in the implementation of the Act.

### National Competent Authorities

Each EU Member State must designate one or more **national competent authorities** and a **national market surveillance authority** to supervise the application and implementation of the AI Act at the national level. These authorities:

- Undertake most compliance investigations and enforcement actions for AI systems (other than GPAI models).
- Operate AI regulatory sandboxes.
- Handle complaints from natural persons.
- Coordinate with the AI Office and the Board.

Member States were required to designate these authorities and adopt national laws on penalties by **2 August 2025**.

### AI Regulatory Sandboxes (Articles 57-58)

Each Member State must establish at least one **AI regulatory sandbox** by **2 August 2026**. These sandboxes are controlled environments that:

- Allow AI systems to be developed, tested, and validated for a limited time before market placement, under a specific sandbox plan agreed with the competent authority.
- May include testing in real-world conditions under supervision.
- Provide guidance, supervision, and support to identify risks, test mitigation measures, and assess compliance.
- Offer particular support to SMEs and startups, including access to guidance on implementing the regulation and value-added services such as help with standardization, certification, and testing.

If an AI system is successfully tested in a sandbox, the provider may use the results as evidence of compliance with the regulation.

## Penalties and Fines

The AI Act establishes a **tiered penalty system** with fines calibrated to the severity of the violation. Penalties are set out in Articles 99 and 101.

| Type of Violation | Maximum Fine |
|---|---|
| **Prohibited AI practices** (Article 5 violations) | Up to **EUR 35 million** or **7%** of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher |
| **High-risk AI system requirements** and other key obligations | Up to **EUR 15 million** or **3%** of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher |
| **GPAI model obligations** (for Commission-imposed fines) | Up to **EUR 15 million** or **3%** of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher |
| **Supply of incorrect, incomplete, or misleading information** to notified bodies or national competent authorities | Up to **EUR 7.5 million** or **1%** of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher |

For **SMEs, including startups**, fines are capped at the lower of the percentage or the absolute amount, providing a degree of proportionality. The Act also requires that fines be **effective, proportionate, and dissuasive**, and that each case be assessed on its individual merits, taking into account factors such as the nature, gravity, and duration of the infringement, and whether the provider or deployer is an SME.

National market surveillance authorities enforce penalties related to AI systems, while the **Commission (through the AI Office)** has exclusive power to impose fines on GPAI model providers. Penalties for GPAI model obligations take effect from **2 August 2026**, while penalties for prohibited practices became enforceable from **2 February 2025**.

## Implementation Timeline

The EU AI Act follows a **phased implementation approach**, with different provisions becoming applicable at staggered intervals following the regulation's entry into force on 1 August 2024.

| Date | Provisions Becoming Applicable |
|---|---|
| **1 August 2024** | AI Act enters into force |
| **2 February 2025** (6 months) | Prohibitions on unacceptable-risk AI practices (Article 5); AI literacy obligations for all providers and deployers |
| **2 August 2025** (12 months) | Obligations for providers of GPAI models (Chapter V); Governance rules (AI Board, Scientific Panel, Advisory Forum); Member States must designate national competent authorities and adopt national penalty laws; Confidentiality rules |
| **2 August 2026** (24 months) | **Majority of the AI Act's rules become applicable**, including: all high-risk AI system obligations for systems listed in Annex III; transparency obligations (Article 50); penalties for GPAI model violations; right to explanation; obligations on deployers; obligations for notified bodies; Member States must establish at least one AI regulatory sandbox |
| **2 August 2027** (36 months) | Full applicability of remaining provisions, including obligations for high-risk AI systems that are safety components of products under Annex I (sectoral product legislation); Existing GPAI models placed on the market before 2 August 2025 must be in full compliance |

## Impact on AI Companies

The EU AI Act has substantial implications for major technology companies developing and deploying AI systems globally.

### OpenAI

[OpenAI](/wiki/openai) has indicated its willingness to comply with the EU AI Act and was among the early signatories of the GPAI Code of Practice. OpenAI published a primer on the EU AI Act stating that signing the Code "reinforces industry-leading safety and transparency measures" the company has pioneered. Given that GPT-4 and subsequent models exceed the 10^25 FLOPs threshold, OpenAI's models are presumed to carry systemic risk, subjecting them to the most stringent obligations under the Act, including adversarial testing, incident reporting, and cybersecurity requirements.

### Google / Alphabet

Google signed the EU AI Code of Practice and has established dedicated compliance infrastructure. Google Cloud published guidance on EU AI Act compliance for its customers. Google's Gemini models, particularly Gemini 1.5 Pro and later versions, are classified or presumed to be classified as GPAI models with systemic risk. However, Google's Kent Walker acknowledged concerns that the Code "risks slowing down Europe's development and deployment of AI."

### Meta

[Meta](/wiki/meta_ai_company) stands as the most prominent holdout, being the only major AI company to **refuse to sign** the GPAI Code of Practice. Meta's Chief Global Affairs Officer characterized the Code as "overreach" and warned that the requirements would stifle AI innovation across Europe. Meta's open-source approach to its [LLaMA](/wiki/llama) models creates additional complexities, as open-source GPAI models with systemic risk do not qualify for exemptions under the Act.

### Anthropic

[Anthropic](/wiki/anthropic) signed the GPAI Code of Practice and has been publicly supportive of the regulatory framework. Anthropic's [Claude](/wiki/claude_ai) models, particularly Claude 3.7 Sonnet and later versions, are among those presumed to pose systemic risk under the Act.

### Microsoft

Microsoft signed the Code of Practice and has committed to compliance. As both a provider (through its own AI products) and a deployer (through its integration of OpenAI models into products like Bing and Microsoft 365 Copilot), Microsoft faces obligations from multiple angles under the AI Act.

### Broader Industry Impact

Companies that sign the Code of Practice gain clearer regulatory pathways and potentially faster market access in the EU, while non-signatories face greater regulatory uncertainty but maintain maximum development flexibility. The **Brussels Effect** means that, in practice, many non-EU companies will adopt EU-compliant practices globally rather than maintaining separate product versions for different markets.

## Comparison with Other Global AI Regulations

The EU AI Act exists within a broader landscape of emerging AI governance frameworks worldwide. Different jurisdictions have adopted markedly different approaches.

| Jurisdiction | Approach | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **European Union** | Comprehensive horizontal legislation (EU AI Act) | Risk-based classification; binding obligations; broad extraterritorial scope; tiered penalties; dedicated enforcement body (AI Office) |
| **United States** | Sector-specific, market-driven | No comprehensive federal AI law; reliance on executive orders (e.g., Biden's October 2023 EO on AI), voluntary commitments, and existing regulatory agencies; state-level laws (e.g., Colorado AI Act); emphasis on innovation |
| **China** | Vertical, technology-specific regulations | Multiple targeted regulations on algorithmic recommendation (2022), deep synthesis / deepfakes (2023), and generative AI (2023); emphasis on content control and national security; approval requirements for public-facing generative AI |
| **United Kingdom** | Pro-innovation, sector-based approach | No single comprehensive AI statute; a "pro-innovation" framework delegating regulation to existing sector regulators (e.g., FCA, Ofcom, CMA); published AI Safety Institute research; voluntary adherence to principles |
| **Canada** | Proposed legislation | Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA), proposed as Part 3 of Bill C-27; risk-based approach similar to EU; not yet enacted as of early 2026 |
| **Brazil** | Proposed legislation | AI regulatory framework under consideration; risk-based classification approach inspired by the EU AI Act |
| **Japan** | Soft law / guidelines | Non-binding AI governance guidelines; emphasis on social principles for trustworthy AI; sector-specific guidance |

### Key Differences

**Scope**: The EU AI Act is the only jurisdiction with a single, comprehensive, horizontally applicable AI law. The United States and China rely on multiple targeted instruments rather than one overarching statute. The UK explicitly rejected the EU's approach in favor of sector-based regulation.

**Prohibited practices**: Both the EU and China prohibit certain AI practices (such as mass surveillance through social scoring and certain uses of emotion recognition), although their specific prohibitions differ substantially. The United States has no federal-level prohibitions on AI applications.

**Enforcement**: The EU created a dedicated enforcement body (the AI Office) with exclusive authority over GPAI models. China enforces AI regulations through its Cyberspace Administration and other existing agencies. The US relies on existing agencies like the FTC, EEOC, and sector regulators.

**Open-source treatment**: The EU AI Act's approach to open-source AI has been particularly debated, as it provides limited exemptions for open-source GPAI models but no exemptions for open-source models with systemic risk. Neither the US nor China has comparable provisions specifically addressing open-source AI.

## Criticism and Debate

The EU AI Act has been subject to significant criticism and debate from industry, academia, civil society, and policymakers.

### Industry Concerns

**Innovation and competitiveness**: A coalition of European company CEOs called for a **two-year "clock-stop"** on implementation in early 2025, citing concerns that the Act's complexity would negatively impact Europe's economic competitiveness. Critics argue that heavy regulatory burdens on AI development could drive AI companies and talent away from Europe, further widening the gap with the United States and China.

**Compliance costs**: Organizations, particularly SMEs and startups, have expressed concern about the significant compliance costs associated with meeting the Act's requirements, especially for high-risk AI systems that require conformity assessments, technical documentation, quality management systems, and ongoing monitoring.

**Trade secrets**: Companies worry that requirements for detailed information about training data, including documentation of how it was obtained lawfully, could jeopardize proprietary information and trade secrets. The tension between transparency and intellectual property protection remains unresolved.

**Technological neutrality**: Some industry representatives argue that the Act's classification system may not adapt quickly enough to rapid technological change, potentially rendering certain provisions outdated or poorly suited to emerging AI capabilities.

### Academic and Civil Society Concerns

**Insufficient protections**: Civil society organizations have argued that the Act does not go far enough in certain areas, particularly regarding the exceptions allowed for real-time biometric identification by law enforcement and the relatively narrow definition of prohibited practices. Some advocacy groups pushed for broader bans on predictive policing and biometric surveillance.

**Definitional challenges**: Academics have criticized the Act's definitions, arguing that the definition of AI systems may become quickly outdated due to the rapid pace of technological development. Some scholars advocate for definitions based on the **properties or effects** of systems rather than their underlying techniques.

**Enforcement capacity**: There are concerns about whether EU and national authorities will have sufficient resources, technical expertise, and institutional capacity to effectively enforce the regulation across such a broad range of AI applications and actors.

**Fundamental rights framing**: Some academic critiques focus on how the Act frames possible harms primarily in terms of fundamental rights, arguing that this creates "communicative paradoxes" and may not adequately capture all types of AI-related harms, including environmental impacts, labor displacement, and concentrations of economic power.

### Debate over Foundation Models / GPAI

The treatment of foundation models (GPAI models) was one of the most **contentious issues** during the legislative process. The original 2021 Commission proposal did not explicitly address foundation models, as systems like [ChatGPT](/wiki/chatgpt) and other [generative AI](/wiki/generative_model) products had not yet become widely known. The rapid proliferation of these systems in late 2022 and 2023 forced lawmakers to integrate provisions on GPAI into the risk framework during trilogue negotiations.

France, Germany, and Italy initially resisted strict regulation of foundation models, concerned about the impact on European AI companies such as Mistral AI and Aleph Alpha. The compromise reached in December 2023 established a two-tier system for GPAI models (general obligations for all, additional obligations for those with systemic risk), which some critics view as insufficiently rigorous and others view as excessively burdensome.

### Extraterritorial Enforcement Challenges

The Act's broad extraterritorial scope raises practical enforcement challenges. While the regulation applies to any provider whose AI system output is used in the EU, enforcing compliance against companies with no physical presence in Europe may prove difficult in practice, particularly for smaller companies that may lack the resources to appoint authorized representatives.

### The "Patchwork" Risk

Although the AI Act is designed to harmonize AI regulation across the EU, the reliance on national competent authorities for enforcement of most provisions (other than GPAI rules) creates a risk of divergent interpretation and enforcement across Member States, potentially leading to a regulatory "patchwork" similar to what has been observed with GDPR enforcement.

## Relationship with Other EU Legislation

The EU AI Act does not operate in isolation. It is designed to complement and interact with several other pieces of EU legislation:

- **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)**: The AI Act explicitly preserves the applicability of the GDPR. AI systems processing personal data must comply with both frameworks. The GDPR's principles of data minimization, purpose limitation, and individual rights remain fully applicable.
- **Digital Services Act (DSA)**: The DSA regulates online intermediaries and platforms. AI systems used by platforms for content moderation, recommendation algorithms, and online advertising must comply with both the DSA and the AI Act.
- **Digital Markets Act (DMA)**: The DMA targets gatekeepers in core platform services. AI systems used by designated gatekeepers are subject to obligations under both frameworks.
- **Product Safety Legislation**: The AI Act's high-risk classification for safety components of products (Annex I) directly links to existing EU product safety directives and regulations, ensuring that AI-specific requirements complement sector-specific safety regimes.
- **EU Liability Directives**: The Commission has proposed complementary legislation on AI liability, including an update to the Product Liability Directive and a proposed AI Liability Directive, to address civil liability for damages caused by AI systems.
