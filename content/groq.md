**Groq, Inc.** is an American [artificial intelligence](/wiki/artificial_intelligence) hardware company that designs and manufactures the Language Processing Unit (LPU), a custom [application-specific integrated circuit](/index.php?title=Application-specific_integrated_circuit&action=edit&redlink=1) (ASIC) optimized for [AI inference](/index.php?title=Inference_(machine_learning)&action=edit&redlink=1). Founded in 2016 by Jonathan Ross and Douglas Wightman, Groq is headquartered in Mountain View, California. The company gained widespread attention in early 2024 for its record-breaking inference speeds on [large language models](/wiki/large_language_model), and in December 2025 entered into a landmark $20 billion licensing and acqui-hire agreement with [NVIDIA](/wiki/nvidia). The company should not be confused with Grok, the AI chatbot developed by [xAI](/index.php?title=XAI_(company)&action=edit&redlink=1).

## History

### Founding and Early Development (2016-2019)

Groq was founded in 2016 by Jonathan Ross and Douglas Wightman, both former engineers at [Google](/index.php?title=Google&action=edit&redlink=1). Ross is credited as one of the original inventors of Google's [Tensor Processing Unit](/index.php?title=Tensor_Processing_Unit&action=edit&redlink=1) (TPU), a custom ASIC designed to accelerate [machine learning](/wiki/machine_learning) workloads. His experience developing the TPU informed his vision for Groq: to build a purpose-designed chip architecture that could deliver deterministic, ultra-low-latency inference for AI workloads.

Wightman, a former engineer at [Google X](/index.php?title=X_(company)&action=edit&redlink=1), served as the company's first CEO before Ross assumed the role. The company received early seed funding from Social Capital, led by Chamath Palihapitiya, with a $10 million investment in 2017. Groq also raised a $10.3 million Series A round in December 2016.

During its early years, Groq focused on designing its chip architecture from the ground up. Rather than adapting existing [GPU](/index.php?title=Graphics_processing_unit&action=edit&redlink=1) or [CPU](/index.php?title=Central_processing_unit&action=edit&redlink=1) architectures for AI workloads, the team built a fundamentally new processor design called the Tensor Streaming Processor (TSP), codenamed "Alan."

### TSP to LPU Rebranding (2020-2023)

The original chip architecture was introduced publicly as the Tensor Streaming Processor (TSP). Following the explosive popularity of [ChatGPT](/wiki/chatgpt) and the widespread adoption of large language models beginning in late 2022, Groq rebranded the TSP as the Language Processing Unit (LPU). The renaming, led by Mark Heaps (VP of Brand) and Jonathan Ross, was intended to make the processor's primary use case more obvious to customers and investors in an AI market increasingly dominated by language model workloads.

### Series C Funding and Unicorn Status (2021)

In April 2021, Groq raised $300 million in a Series C round led by [Tiger Global Management](/index.php?title=Tiger_Global_Management&action=edit&redlink=1) and D1 Capital Partners. This round valued the company at over $1 billion, making Groq a [unicorn](/index.php?title=Unicorn_(finance)&action=edit&redlink=1). The funds were used to scale chip manufacturing and expand the company's engineering team.

### GroqCloud Launch and Viral Growth (2024)

On February 19, 2024, Groq soft-launched GroqCloud, a developer platform that provided API access to its LPU-powered inference infrastructure. The timing proved fortuitous. Developers and AI researchers were increasingly looking for alternatives to [NVIDIA](/wiki/nvidia) [GPU](/index.php?title=Graphics_processing_unit&action=edit&redlink=1)-based inference, and Groq's dramatically faster response times generated significant attention on social media and in the technology press.

The company's demo, which showed Groq generating responses from [Meta](/wiki/meta)'s [Llama](/wiki/llama) models at hundreds of tokens per second (compared to tens of tokens per second on GPU-based platforms), went viral. This performance advantage, driven by the LPU architecture, positioned Groq as the leading inference speed provider in the industry.

### Series D Funding (2024)

In August 2024, Groq raised $640 million in a Series D round led by BlackRock Private Equity Partners, valuing the company at $2.8 billion. The funds were directed toward expanding inference infrastructure and scaling GroqCloud to meet the growing demand from developers and enterprises.

### Saudi Arabia Investment (2025)

On February 10, 2025, Groq announced that it had secured a $1.5 billion commitment from the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia (through HUMAIN, the Saudi AI infrastructure entity) to expand delivery of its LPU-based AI inference infrastructure in the region. This agreement marked one of the largest international AI infrastructure investments and underscored growing global demand for high-performance inference capabilities.

### Series E Funding (2025)

In September 2025, Groq announced $750 million in new financing at a post-money valuation of $6.9 billion. The round reflected surging demand for inference infrastructure as enterprises deployed AI models at scale. By this point, Groq had generated approximately $172.5 million in revenue with a team of 624 employees.

### U.S. Department of Energy Partnership (2025)

Groq partnered with the U.S. Department of Energy to advance AI inference capabilities and develop next-generation computing infrastructure. This partnership highlighted the potential of LPU technology for government and scientific computing applications beyond commercial AI.

### NVIDIA Licensing and Acqui-Hire Agreement (December 2025)

On December 24, 2025, [NVIDIA](/wiki/nvidia) announced a landmark agreement with Groq, reportedly valued at approximately $20 billion. This represented NVIDIA's largest deal on record, surpassing its 2019 acquisition of Mellanox for approximately $7 billion.

The deal was structured as a non-exclusive licensing and acqui-hire arrangement rather than a traditional acquisition. Under the terms:

- NVIDIA received a non-exclusive license to Groq's inference technology, including the LPU architecture and related intellectual property.
- Jonathan Ross (Founder and CEO), Sunny Madra (President), and other senior Groq leaders joined NVIDIA to help advance and scale the licensed technology.
- Groq remained a nominally independent legal entity, allowing the deal to proceed without the extensive antitrust review that a full acquisition would have triggered.

The transaction reflected NVIDIA's strategic recognition that inference workloads (as opposed to training) were becoming the dominant use case for AI compute, and that Groq's deterministic architecture offered capabilities that NVIDIA's GPU-based solutions could not easily replicate.

### Groq 3 LPU (2026)

At [NVIDIA](/wiki/nvidia)'s GTC 2026 conference in March 2026, the company unveiled the Groq 3 LPU (also referred to as LPX), designed for the NVIDIA Vera Rubin platform. The Groq 3 LPU targets agentic AI inference at 1,500 tokens per second, with 500 megabytes of SRAM memory and 1.2 petaFLOPS of 8-bit computation. Its memory bandwidth reaches 150 TB/s, approximately seven times faster than the Rubin GPU's 22 TB/s. This announcement demonstrated how Groq's technology was being integrated into NVIDIA's broader hardware ecosystem.

## Technology

### LPU Architecture

The Language Processing Unit (LPU) represents a fundamental departure from the [GPU](/index.php?title=Graphics_processing_unit&action=edit&redlink=1)-based architectures that dominate AI compute. While GPUs were originally designed for parallel graphics rendering and later adapted for AI workloads, the LPU was designed from the ground up specifically for the linear algebra calculations that are the primary requirement for AI inference.

#### Design Principles

The LPU architecture is built on several core principles:

**Software-First Design**: The LPU was designed with the principle that all execution should be explicitly controlled by the compiler rather than reactive hardware components. This eliminates the need for traditional CPU/GPU components such as branch predictors, arbiters, reordering buffers, and caches, which introduce non-deterministic behavior and overhead.

**Deterministic Execution**: Because the compiler controls all execution explicitly, the LPU can guarantee deterministic execution of programs. This predictability enables precise performance guarantees and simplifies system design, especially for latency-sensitive applications.

**On-Chip SRAM**: The LPU integrates hundreds of megabytes of SRAM as primary weight storage (not cache). This design choice eliminates the latency associated with accessing external [DRAM](/index.php?title=DRAM&action=edit&redlink=1) and ensures that compute units are fed at full speed. Traditional GPUs rely on high-bandwidth memory (HBM) chips, which, while fast, introduce higher latency than on-chip SRAM.

**Vector Processing**: At its core, the LPU is a very large vector processor. The fundamental unit of both computation and communication is a 320-element vector consisting of 320 bytes at INT8 precision or 640 bytes at FP16 precision.

#### TruePoint Precision

Groq developed a proprietary precision format called TruePoint, which reduces numerical precision only in areas that do not affect model accuracy. The format stores 100 bits of intermediate accumulation, providing sufficient range and precision to guarantee lossless accumulation regardless of input bit width. This approach allows the LPU to achieve high throughput while maintaining model quality.

### Chip Generations

| Generation | Process Node | Key Specifications | Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| LPU v1 (TSP) | 14nm | 750 TOPS INT8, 230MB SRAM, 80TB/s internal bandwidth, 25x29mm die, 900MHz | Production |
| LPU v2 | Samsung 4nm | Next-generation performance improvements | In development (announced August 2023) |
| Groq 3 LPU (LPX) | N/A | 1.2 PFLOPS INT8, 500MB SRAM, 150TB/s bandwidth, 1,500 tokens/s target | Announced at GTC 2026 |

The first-generation LPU chip achieves a computational density of more than 1 TeraOp/s per square millimeter of silicon. The chip was manufactured on a 14nm process node with a die size of 25x29mm, operating at a nominal clock frequency of 900 MHz.

For the second-generation chip, Groq selected Samsung Electronics' foundry in Taylor, Texas, in August 2023, with manufacturing planned on Samsung's 4nm process node.

## Groq vs. Grok: Naming Confusion

Groq is frequently confused with [Grok](/index.php?title=Grok_(chatbot)&action=edit&redlink=1), the AI chatbot developed by [Elon Musk](/index.php?title=Elon_Musk&action=edit&redlink=1)'s company [xAI](/index.php?title=XAI_(company)&action=edit&redlink=1). Despite the near-identical pronunciation, the two companies operate in entirely different layers of the AI stack:

| | Groq | Grok |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Company** | Groq, Inc. | [xAI](/index.php?title=XAI_(company)&action=edit&redlink=1) |
| **Founded** | 2016 | 2023 |
| **Product Type** | Inference hardware (LPU chip) | AI chatbot / [large language model](/wiki/large_language_model) |
| **Layer** | Hardware and inference infrastructure | Model and application |
| **Use Case** | Running any AI model at high speed | Conversational AI assistant on [X](/index.php?title=X_(social_network)&action=edit&redlink=1) |

Groq trademarked its name in 2016, several years before xAI launched Grok in November 2023. In response to the naming similarity, Groq sent a cease-and-desist letter to xAI, citing potential trademark infringement. Groq argued that the single-letter difference between the names was insufficient to avoid market confusion. xAI responded by filing its own trademark applications for "GROK" with the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) in December 2023.

The naming dispute drew significant public attention, with Groq publishing a blog post titled "Hey Elon, It's Time To Cease & De-grok." Despite the similar names, the two products are complementary rather than competitive: Grok (the model) could theoretically run on Groq (the hardware).
