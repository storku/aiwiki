**AI in law** (also called legal AI or legal technology/legaltech) refers to the application of [artificial intelligence](/wiki/artificial_intelligence) technologies to legal practice, including document review, legal research, contract analysis, predictive analytics, and compliance monitoring. The legal AI software market was estimated at $3.11 billion in 2025 and is projected to reach $10.82 billion by 2030 at a compound annual growth rate of 28.3%. A Thomson Reuters survey from April 2025 found that 26% of legal organizations were actively using [generative AI](/wiki/generative_ai), up from 14% in 2024, while 31% of legal professionals reported using generative AI in their daily practice.

## Overview

The integration of AI into legal practice has followed a trajectory from narrow, rule-based applications to sophisticated [large language model](/wiki/large_language_model) (LLM) systems capable of understanding legal reasoning, drafting documents, and conducting multi-step research. Early legal technology focused on keyword search and document management, but the field has been transformed by advances in [natural language processing](/wiki/natural_language_processing), [machine learning](/wiki/machine_learning), and generative AI.

The legal profession presents both compelling opportunities and unique challenges for AI adoption. On one hand, legal work involves enormous volumes of text, precedent analysis, and pattern recognition, all tasks where AI excels. On the other hand, the adversarial nature of law, the high stakes of legal outcomes, the importance of client confidentiality, and the ethical obligations of attorneys impose stringent requirements on AI reliability and accountability.

2025 was characterized as the year of AI deployment in the legal industry, with "agentic AI" emerging as the dominant theme in product announcements. Agentic AI systems go beyond simple question-answering to execute multi-step legal workflows autonomously, such as conducting comprehensive legal research, drafting documents, and managing case workflows with minimal human intervention.

## Document Review and E-Discovery

Document review and [electronic discovery](/wiki/electronic_discovery) (e-discovery) represent some of the earliest and most mature applications of AI in law. E-discovery involves identifying, collecting, and reviewing electronically stored information (ESI) relevant to legal proceedings. In complex litigation, parties may need to review millions of documents, making AI-assisted review essential for managing costs and timelines.

### Technology-Assisted Review (TAR)

Technology-assisted review uses [machine learning](/wiki/machine_learning) algorithms to classify documents as relevant or non-relevant based on human-coded training examples. TAR systems learn from attorney decisions and apply those patterns to the remaining document population, significantly reducing the volume of documents requiring manual review.

### Impact on Legal Practice

AI-powered document review has dramatically reduced the time and cost of e-discovery. Tasks that once required teams of contract attorneys working for weeks can now be completed in days. Studies have shown that technology-assisted review often achieves higher accuracy than manual review alone, as human reviewers suffer from fatigue and inconsistency over long review sessions.

## Legal Research Assistants

AI-powered legal research tools represent one of the most rapidly evolving segments of legal technology. These platforms use [large language models](/wiki/large_language_model) to understand natural language queries, analyze case law, identify relevant statutes and regulations, and generate research summaries grounded in authoritative legal sources.

## Predictive Analytics for Case Outcomes

Predictive analytics in law uses AI and [machine learning](/wiki/machine_learning) algorithms to analyze large datasets of past case rulings, legal filings, judicial decisions, and jury behavior to predict future legal outcomes and guide litigation strategy.

### Leading Predictive Analytics Platforms

| Platform | Data Source | Accuracy | Key Capabilities |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre/Dicta | 20 years of federal case data; profiles of 1,000+ judges | 85% accuracy in forecasting judicial rulings on dispositive motions | Judge behavior analysis, motion outcome prediction, case strategy recommendations |
| Lex Machina (LexisNexis) | Federal and state court data across multiple practice areas | 70-80% accuracy depending on practice area | Judge analytics, case outcome prediction, timing analysis, damages assessment |
| Ravel Law (acquired by LexisNexis) | Judicial analytics and case law visualization | Varies by dataset | Visual mapping of legal arguments and judicial reasoning patterns |

According to 2025 data, 93% of mid-sized law firms are using AI in some capacity, with 51% reporting widespread adoption. Modern AI predictive tools can deliver up to 80-90% accuracy by analyzing historical case data, judge behavior, and jurisdiction-specific trends.

### Applications of Predictive Analytics

Predictive analytics tools serve several key functions in legal practice:

- **Litigation strategy**: Helping lawyers assess the likely outcome of motions, trials, and appeals before investing time and resources.
- **Case valuation**: Estimating the potential value of a case for settlement purposes. One employment law firm reported that using predictive analytics for case valuation increased their settlement success rate by 34% while reducing time to settlement by an average of three months.
- **Judge profiling**: Analyzing judicial behavior patterns, including how specific judges rule on particular motion types, their typical timelines, and their receptiveness to certain legal arguments.
- **Budget forecasting**: Providing data-driven cost estimates for litigation based on comparable case histories.
- **Risk management**: Helping corporate legal departments assess and manage legal risk across their organizations.

## AI Hallucination Risks in Legal Filings

One of the most significant risks associated with AI in legal practice is the problem of hallucination, where AI systems generate plausible-sounding but factually incorrect information, including fabricated case citations, fictitious judicial opinions, and invented legal arguments.

### Mata v. Avianca (2023)

The most prominent example of AI hallucination in legal proceedings is *Mata v. Avianca, Inc.*, a case in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York that became a cautionary tale for the legal profession.

**Background**: In February 2022, Roberto Mata filed a personal injury lawsuit against the airline Avianca, alleging he was injured when a metal serving cart struck his knee during a flight. The case itself was routine, but the legal proceedings became extraordinary due to the attorneys' use of ChatGPT.

**The AI-generated brief**: Attorneys Peter LoDuca and Steven A. Schwartz of the firm Levidow, Levidow & Oberman P.C. used ChatGPT to prepare a legal brief opposing Avianca's motion to dismiss. The brief cited numerous case authorities, including cases such as *Varghese v. China Southern Airlines Co., Ltd.*, *Martinez v. Delta Air Lines, Inc.*, and *Zicherman v. Korean Air Lines Co., Ltd.* The problem was that several of these cases were entirely fabricated by ChatGPT. The AI had generated convincing-sounding case names, citations, and even quotations from judicial opinions that did not exist.

**Discovery and sanctions**: Avianca's attorneys notified the court that they could not locate several of the cited cases. Judge P. Kevin Castel ordered the plaintiff's attorneys to provide copies. Rather than immediately withdrawing the brief, the attorneys initially attempted to verify the cases by asking ChatGPT whether they were real (ChatGPT confirmed they were, compounding the error). Ultimately, Judge Castel issued a $5,000 fine and sanctioned both attorneys for their "acts of conscious avoidance and false and misleading statements to the Court."

**Impact on the profession**: The case received widespread media attention and prompted courts, bar associations, and law firms to establish guidelines for AI use in legal practice. Multiple federal courts subsequently adopted standing orders requiring attorneys to certify that no AI-generated content was submitted without human verification.

### Broader Pattern of AI Failures

The *Mata v. Avianca* case was not an isolated incident. Subsequent cases have involved attorneys submitting AI-generated briefs with fabricated citations, suggesting that the problem extends beyond a single instance of attorney negligence. These incidents have been described as an "epidemic of AI failures in law," prompting systematic responses from courts and professional organizations.

## Regulatory and Ethical Considerations

The use of AI in legal practice raises numerous regulatory and ethical questions, ranging from professional responsibility obligations to broader societal concerns about access to justice and algorithmic bias.

### Professional Responsibility

Lawyers using AI tools must navigate their existing ethical obligations, which were developed long before the advent of generative AI:

| Ethical Obligation | Relevance to AI Use |
|---|---|
| Competence (ABA Model Rule 1.1) | Lawyers must understand the benefits and risks of the technologies they use, including AI tools. This requires sufficient knowledge of how AI works, its limitations, and the risk of hallucination. |
| Confidentiality (ABA Model Rule 1.6) | Client information entered into AI systems may be stored, processed, or used for training. Lawyers must ensure that AI use does not compromise client confidentiality. |
| Communication (ABA Model Rule 1.4) | Lawyers should inform clients about the use of AI in their matter and obtain consent where appropriate. |
| Candor toward the tribunal (ABA Model Rule 3.3) | Lawyers are responsible for verifying all AI-generated citations and legal arguments before submitting them to a court. |
| Supervision (ABA Model Rules 5.1, 5.3) | Partners and supervising attorneys must ensure that those under their supervision use AI tools appropriately. |
| Reasonable fees (ABA Model Rule 1.5) | If AI reduces the time required to complete a task, lawyers may only bill for the time actually spent. A task that takes 15 minutes with AI cannot be billed as three hours of manual work. |

### Algorithmic Bias

AI systems trained on historical legal data risk perpetuating existing biases in the legal system. When AI models learn from historical judicial decisions that reflect systemic biases (racial, socioeconomic, or otherwise), they can reproduce and even amplify those biases. This is particularly concerning in areas such as:

- **Criminal sentencing**: Risk assessment tools used in bail and sentencing decisions have been shown to exhibit racial bias.
- **Hiring discrimination**: AI tools used in legal hiring may perpetuate demographic biases present in training data.
- **Case outcome prediction**: Predictive models may reflect historical patterns of unequal justice.

Complex "black box" models that cannot explain their decisions are considered fundamentally unsuitable for legal applications, where transparency is a cornerstone of justice. AI systems deployed in criminal justice or employment law must be explainable, generating human-readable reasons for their recommendations.

### Data Privacy and Confidentiality

Legal professionals handle some of the most sensitive information in any profession. The use of cloud-based AI tools introduces risks related to data security, unauthorized access, and potential use of client data for AI model training. Firms must carefully evaluate the data handling practices of AI vendors and implement appropriate safeguards, including:

- Data processing agreements with AI providers
- Encryption and access controls
- Restrictions on using client data in AI training
- Compliance with regulations such as GDPR, CCPA, and sector-specific rules

### The EU AI Act

The [European Union's AI Act](/wiki/eu_ai_act), which began taking effect in stages from 2024, classifies AI systems used in law enforcement and the administration of justice as "high-risk," subjecting them to enhanced requirements for transparency, human oversight, and accuracy. This regulation has implications for legal AI tools used in European jurisdictions.

## Bar Association Guidance

Professional bar associations have responded to the rapid adoption of AI in legal practice by issuing formal ethics opinions and guidance documents.

### ABA Formal Opinion 512

The American Bar Association (ABA) Standing Committee on Ethics and Professional Responsibility released Formal Opinion 512 on July 29, 2024. This opinion addresses the use of generative AI in legal practice through the lens of existing Model Rules of Professional Conduct.

Key points from Opinion 512:

- Lawyers have a duty of competence that extends to understanding the AI tools they use.
- Confidential client information should not be entered into AI systems without appropriate safeguards.
- Lawyers must verify all AI-generated work product, particularly legal citations and case analysis.
- Fee arrangements must reflect the actual time spent, not the time that would have been required without AI.
- Supervising attorneys bear responsibility for ensuring that junior lawyers and staff use AI appropriately.

### ABA Task Force on AI (2025)

The ABA's Task Force on AI released its Year 2 report in December 2025, titled "Addressing the Legal Challenges of AI." The report concluded that "AI has moved from experiment to infrastructure for the legal profession," marking a pivotal moment in the profession's relationship with technology.

### State-Level Guidance

Individual state bar associations have also issued guidance:

| Jurisdiction | Guidance |
|---|---|
| Texas State Bar (Opinion No. 705, February 2025) | Specific guidance on lawyers' use of generative AI, addressing competence, confidentiality, and supervisory obligations |
| Washington State Bar (Advisory Opinion 2025-05, 2025) | Guidance on ethical obligations when using AI in legal practice |
| California State Bar | Ethics opinions on AI use in client communications and legal research |
| Florida Bar | Adopted ABA Opinion 512 framework with state-specific considerations |
| New York courts | Multiple standing orders requiring disclosure of AI use in court filings |

### Federal Court Standing Orders

Following the *Mata v. Avianca* case, numerous federal judges adopted standing orders addressing AI use in legal filings. These orders typically require attorneys to:

- Disclose the use of AI tools in preparing court submissions
- Certify that all citations and legal authorities have been verified by a human attorney
- Accept professional responsibility for all content submitted to the court, regardless of whether it was generated by AI
