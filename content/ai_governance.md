**AI governance** refers to the frameworks, policies, regulations, and institutional arrangements designed to guide the development, deployment, and use of [artificial intelligence](/wiki/artificial_intelligence) systems in a manner that is safe, ethical, and beneficial to society. As AI technologies have grown more powerful and pervasive, governance has emerged as one of the most actively debated areas in technology policy, involving governments, international organizations, industry, academia, and civil society. Key governance frameworks include the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, the OECD AI Principles, UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence, the EU AI Act, and the establishment of national AI Safety Institutes.

## Overview

AI governance encompasses a broad range of activities and institutions, from high-level principles and international agreements to specific regulatory requirements and technical standards. The field has evolved rapidly since the mid-2010s, driven by growing awareness of AI's transformative potential and the risks associated with insufficiently governed AI systems.

The core challenge of AI governance is balancing innovation with risk management. Overly restrictive governance may slow beneficial AI development, while insufficient governance may allow harmful applications to proliferate unchecked. Finding the right balance requires coordination across multiple stakeholders and jurisdictions, accounting for widely varying cultural, economic, and political contexts.

### Key Dimensions of AI Governance

| Dimension | Description | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Risk management | Identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks from AI systems | NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act risk categories |
| Ethics and values | Embedding human rights, fairness, and accountability into AI development | OECD AI Principles, UNESCO Recommendation |
| Technical standards | Developing interoperable standards for AI safety, testing, and evaluation | ISO/IEC 42001, IEEE standards |
| Institutional design | Creating bodies to oversee, evaluate, and enforce AI governance | AI Safety Institutes, regulatory agencies |
| International cooperation | Coordinating governance across national borders | G7 Hiroshima Process, OECD.AI, AI Safety Institute network |
| Corporate governance | Internal policies and practices for responsible AI within organizations | [Responsible AI](/wiki/responsible_ai) frameworks, ethics boards |

## NIST AI Risk Management Framework

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) released the AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) on January 26, 2023. Developed over an 18-month period in collaboration with more than 240 contributing organizations from private industry, academia, civil society, and government, the framework is designed to help organizations manage risks associated with AI systems throughout their lifecycle.

### Design Principles

The AI RMF is:

- **Voluntary**: Not a regulatory requirement, but a guidance document that organizations can adopt and adapt
- **Rights-preserving**: Designed to support the protection of civil liberties and human rights
- **Non-sector-specific**: Applicable across industries and use cases
- **Use-case agnostic**: Flexible enough to apply to diverse AI applications
- **Iterative**: Intended to be applied continuously throughout the AI lifecycle, not just at a single point

### Core Structure

The AI RMF is organized around four core functions:

| Function | Description | Key Activities |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **GOVERN** | Establishes the organizational context and culture for AI risk management | Risk management policies, roles and responsibilities, organizational culture, legal and regulatory requirements |
| **MAP** | Identifies and contextualizes AI system risks | Context mapping, stakeholder identification, risk categorization, impact assessment |
| **MEASURE** | Assesses, analyzes, and tracks identified risks | Metrics development, testing and evaluation, bias assessment, performance monitoring |
| **MANAGE** | Prioritizes and acts on risk mitigation | Risk treatment, resource allocation, incident response, continuous improvement |

The GOVERN function applies to all stages of an organization's AI risk management processes, while MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE can be applied in system-specific contexts and at specific stages of the AI lifecycle.

### Supporting Resources

NIST has published several companion documents:

- **AI RMF Playbook**: Practical guidance for implementing each function and category of the framework
- **AI RMF Roadmap**: A plan for future development and expansion of the framework
- **AI RMF Crosswalks**: Mappings between the AI RMF and other frameworks (including the OECD AI Principles and ISO 42001)
- **AI RMF Profiles**: Sector-specific and use-case-specific guidance (including profiles for [generative AI](/wiki/generative_artificial_intelligence))

### Generative AI Profile (NIST AI 600-1)

In July 2024, NIST released the AI RMF Generative AI Profile (NIST AI 600-1), extending the framework to address risks specific to [generative AI](/wiki/generative_artificial_intelligence) systems, including hallucination, harmful content generation, data privacy, and intellectual property concerns.

## OECD AI Principles

The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) adopted its AI Principles in May 2019, making them the first intergovernmental standard on AI. The principles were subsequently endorsed by G20 leaders and have become one of the most widely referenced international AI governance frameworks, with adherents including all OECD member countries and several partner nations.

### Values-Based Principles

The OECD AI Principles are organized around five value-based pillars:

| Principle | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Inclusive growth, sustainable development, and well-being | AI should benefit people and the planet, contributing to inclusive growth, sustainable development, and well-being |
| Human-centered values and fairness | AI systems should respect the rule of law, human rights, democratic values, and diversity, and should include safeguards to ensure fairness |
| Transparency and explainability | There should be transparency and responsible disclosure around AI systems to ensure people understand AI-based outcomes and can challenge them |
| Robustness, security, and safety | AI systems should function in a robust, secure, and safe way throughout their lifecycle, and potential risks should be continuously assessed and managed |
| Accountability | Organizations and individuals developing, deploying, or operating AI systems should be held accountable for their proper functioning in line with the above principles |

### Policy Recommendations

In addition to the values-based principles, the OECD provides five recommendations for governments:

1. **Investing in AI research and development**: Governments should invest in long-term public and private AI R&D
2. **Fostering a digital ecosystem for AI**: Governments should promote accessible AI ecosystems with enabling digital infrastructure and technologies
3. **Shaping an enabling policy environment for AI**: Governments should create a policy environment that supports an agile transition to AI
4. **Building human capacity and preparing for labor market transformation**: Governments should empower people with skills for AI and support fair labor market transitions
5. **International co-operation for trustworthy AI**: Governments should work together to advance trustworthy AI standards and responsible stewardship

### 2024 Update

The OECD updated its AI Principles in May 2024 to address developments since the original adoption. Key revisions included:

- Clarifying the definition of AI systems to encompass systems that continue to evolve after deployment
- Addressing [generative AI](/wiki/generative_artificial_intelligence) capabilities, including content generation
- Strengthening language around risk management and safety
- Reflecting the emergence of new AI governance institutions, such as AI Safety Institutes

The OECD also integrated with the Global Partnership on AI (GPAI) in 2024, combining their efforts under an "integrated partnership" involving 44 participating governments from seven continents.

### OECD.AI Policy Observatory

The OECD maintains the OECD.AI Policy Observatory, a platform that provides data, analysis, and tools for AI policy. It tracks AI policies across countries, monitors AI incidents, and provides comparative analysis of national AI strategies.

## UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence

UNESCO adopted its Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence in November 2021, making it the first global standard on AI ethics. The Recommendation is applicable to all 194 UNESCO member states and provides a comprehensive ethical framework spanning values, principles, and policy action areas.

### Core Values and Principles

The Recommendation is built on four foundational values:

| Value | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Human rights and human dignity | AI must respect, protect, and promote human rights and fundamental freedoms |
| Living in peaceful, just, and interconnected societies | AI should promote interconnection, inclusion, and equity |
| Ensuring diversity and inclusiveness | AI should promote cultural and social diversity and inclusion |
| Environment and ecosystem flourishing | AI should be developed with consideration for its environmental impact |

These values are operationalized through ten core principles:

1. Proportionality and do no harm
2. Safety and security
3. Fairness and non-discrimination
4. Sustainability
5. Right to privacy and data protection
6. Human oversight and determination
7. Transparency and explainability
8. Responsibility and accountability
9. Awareness and literacy
10. Multi-stakeholder and adaptive governance

### Policy Action Areas

What distinguishes the UNESCO Recommendation from many other frameworks is its extensive Policy Action Areas, which provide concrete guidance for translating values and principles into action across eleven domains:

| Policy Action Area | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Policy and regulation | National AI policies, legal frameworks, and oversight mechanisms |
| Data governance | Data quality, access, privacy, and management |
| Development and international cooperation | Capacity building and knowledge transfer |
| Environment and ecosystems | Environmental impact assessment and sustainable AI |
| Gender | Addressing gender bias in AI systems and ensuring inclusive representation |
| Science, technology, and innovation | Research priorities and open science for AI |
| Communication and information | Media literacy and combating disinformation |
| Education and research | AI literacy, curriculum development, and research ethics |
| Culture | Cultural diversity and heritage preservation |
| Economy and labor | Labor market transformation and economic impact |
| Health and social well-being | AI in healthcare, social services, and well-being |

### Implementation

UNESCO has developed a Readiness Assessment Methodology (RAM) to help member states evaluate their preparedness for implementing the Recommendation. As of 2025, over 50 countries have undergone or initiated readiness assessments, with several producing national action plans based on the results.

## EU AI Act

The European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) is the first comprehensive legal framework for AI regulation in the world. The Act was formally adopted in 2024 and entered into force on August 1, 2024, with a phased implementation timeline extending to August 2027.

### Risk-Based Classification

The EU AI Act establishes a risk-based classification system with four tiers:

| Risk Level | Description | Regulatory Requirements | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Unacceptable | AI systems that pose a fundamental threat to people's safety, livelihoods, and rights | Prohibited | Social scoring by governments, real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces (with limited exceptions), manipulation of vulnerable populations |
| High | AI systems that pose significant risks to health, safety, or fundamental rights | Mandatory conformity assessment, technical documentation, human oversight, transparency | Medical devices, autonomous vehicles, credit scoring, recruitment tools, critical infrastructure management |
| Limited | AI systems that interact with users or generate content | Transparency obligations (users must be informed they are interacting with AI) | Chatbots, [deepfake](/wiki/deepfake_detection) generators, emotion recognition systems |
| Minimal | AI systems that pose negligible risk | No specific obligations | AI-powered video games, spam filters, inventory management |

### Implementation Timeline

| Date | Milestone |
| --- | --- |
| August 1, 2024 | AI Act enters into force |
| February 2, 2025 | Prohibited AI practices and AI literacy obligations take effect |
| August 2, 2025 | Governance rules and obligations for general-purpose AI (GPAI) models become applicable |
| August 2, 2026 | Full applicability of the AI Act |
| August 2, 2027 | Extended transition period for high-risk AI systems embedded in regulated products |

### General-Purpose AI Models

The EU AI Act includes specific provisions for general-purpose AI (GPAI) models, including [large language models](/wiki/large_language_model):

- All GPAI providers must provide technical documentation, comply with EU copyright law, and publish training data summaries
- GPAI models with "systemic risk" (defined by a computational training threshold of 10^25 floating point operations) face additional obligations, including model evaluation, adversarial testing, incident reporting, and cybersecurity measures

### Enforcement

The EU AI Act is enforced through national market surveillance authorities in each EU member state, with the European AI Office coordinating enforcement at the EU level. Penalties for non-compliance can reach up to 35 million euros or 7% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher.

## National AI Safety Institutes

The establishment of national AI Safety Institutes represents one of the most significant institutional developments in AI governance. These bodies are dedicated to understanding and mitigating risks from advanced AI systems through research, evaluation, and testing.

### UK AI Safety Institute (now AI Security Institute)

The United Kingdom established its AI Safety Institute in November 2023, following the UK AI Safety Summit at Bletchley Park. The Institute evolved from the Frontier AI Taskforce and was the world's first government body dedicated to AI safety evaluation.

**Mission**: To minimize surprise to the UK and humanity from rapid and unexpected advances in AI. The Institute advances knowledge of AI safety by examining, evaluating, and testing new types of AI to understand their capabilities and risks.

**Key activities**:
- Pre-deployment testing of frontier AI models (the Institute has agreements with major AI labs to test models before release)
- Fundamental research on AI safety, including evaluation methodologies and risk assessment
- International collaboration through the network of AI Safety Institutes
- Publication of research findings and safety evaluations

**Funding**: The UK AISI received approximately 100 million pounds ($127 million) in public funding, making it one of the best-resourced AI safety bodies globally.

**Name change**: In 2025, the UK government renamed the AI Safety Institute to the "AI Security Institute" (AISI), reflecting a broader mandate that encompasses both safety and security dimensions of advanced AI.

### US AI Safety Institute (now Center for AI Standards and Innovation)

The United States established its AI Safety Institute as part of the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) in November 2023, coinciding with the UK AI Safety Summit.

**Mission**: To equip governments with a scientific understanding of the risks posed by advanced AI, conducting research and developing and testing mitigations.

**Key activities**:
- Developing evaluation methodologies and benchmarks for AI safety
- Coordinating with the AI industry on voluntary safety commitments
- Contributing to international standards development
- Supporting NIST's broader AI risk management work

**Evolution**: In June 2025, the US AI Safety Institute was renamed the Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI), with a transformed mission focused more heavily on standards development and innovation alongside safety evaluation.

### International Network of AI Safety Institutes

During the AI Seoul Summit in May 2024, international leaders agreed to form a network of AI Safety Institutes comprising members from the UK, the US, Japan, France, Germany, Italy, Singapore, South Korea, Australia, Canada, and the European Union.

The network has launched several collaborative projects:

| Project | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Joint Evaluation Protocol (JEP) | Shared methodology for evaluating frontier AI models |
| Global AI Incident Database | Centralized repository of AI safety incidents and near-misses |
| Open Safety Benchmarks Initiative | Development of shared benchmarks for AI safety evaluation |
| Capacity Building Programme | Assistance for countries developing AI safety capabilities |

The inaugural meeting of this network was held in November 2024 in San Francisco, establishing working relationships and shared priorities across participating institutes.

## Corporate AI Governance

Beyond government regulation, major technology companies have developed their own internal AI governance structures. These corporate frameworks vary in their specifics but generally include ethics review processes, safety evaluation protocols, and organizational structures for accountability.

### Anthropic

[Anthropic](/wiki/anthropic) has developed the [Responsible Scaling Policy](/wiki/responsible_ai) (RSP), which defines AI Safety Levels (ASL) as a graduated framework for matching safety measures to model capabilities. The RSP requires increasingly stringent safety and security measures as models become more capable, with formal evaluation gates that must be passed before scaling to the next level.

### OpenAI

[OpenAI](/wiki/openai) operates under its Preparedness Framework, which tracks frontier capabilities across risk categories (biological, cybersecurity, AI self-improvement) and defines two capability thresholds: High (could amplify existing harm pathways) and Critical (could create unprecedented harm pathways). A Safety Advisory Group (SAG) oversees the framework and makes recommendations on safeguards.

### Google DeepMind

[Google](/wiki/google) DeepMind introduced the Frontier Safety Framework in May 2024, which identifies "Critical Capability Levels" (CCLs) representing the minimum capabilities a model must have to contribute to severe harm. The framework requires periodic evaluation of frontier models and defines response protocols when CCLs are reached.

### Microsoft

[Microsoft](/wiki/microsoft) has established an Office of Responsible AI, a Responsible AI Standard (a detailed implementation guide), and an internal review process for high-risk AI deployments. The company's governance structure includes an Aether Committee (Advisory Committee on Ethics and Effects in Engineering and Research) that provides guidance on AI ethics issues.

### Industry Comparisons

| Company | Framework | Key Features | Risk Classification |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| [Anthropic](/wiki/anthropic) | Responsible Scaling Policy | AI Safety Levels (ASL-1 through ASL-4+) | Capability-based (biosecurity, cyber, autonomy) |
| [OpenAI](/wiki/openai) | Preparedness Framework | High and Critical capability thresholds | Domain-based (bio, cyber, self-improvement) |
| Google DeepMind | Frontier Safety Framework | Critical Capability Levels (CCLs) | Capability-based (severe harm pathways) |
| [Microsoft](/wiki/microsoft) | Responsible AI Standard | Six principles, impact assessments | Use-case-based (high-sensitivity applications) |
| Meta | AI safety policies | Community standards, content policies | Application-specific |

## International Cooperation

AI governance increasingly requires international coordination due to the global nature of AI development and deployment. Several multilateral initiatives have emerged.

### G7 Hiroshima AI Process

The G7 launched the Hiroshima AI Process in 2023, which produced:

- International Guiding Principles for Organizations Developing Advanced AI Systems
- A voluntary Code of Conduct for AI developers
- Ongoing work on governance of general-purpose AI and frontier models

### United Nations

The UN has engaged with AI governance through multiple channels:

- The Secretary-General's High-Level Advisory Body on AI published its interim report in December 2023 and final report in September 2024, recommending the establishment of an International Scientific Panel on AI
- UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of AI (discussed above)
- The International Telecommunication Union (ITU) has led work on AI standards through its AI for Good platform

### Bilateral Agreements

Several bilateral AI governance agreements have been established:

- The US-UK partnership on AI safety (including mutual commitments on AI safety testing)
- The US-EU Trade and Technology Council's work on AI governance alignment
- Japan-EU cooperation on AI governance standards

## Challenges in AI Governance

AI governance faces several persistent challenges:

### Pace of Innovation

AI technology advances faster than governance frameworks can adapt. By the time a regulatory framework is finalized, the technology it was designed to govern may have evolved significantly. This "pacing problem" has led to calls for more agile, adaptive governance approaches.

### Definitional Challenges

Defining what constitutes "AI" for regulatory purposes remains contentious. Broad definitions risk capturing simple software systems, while narrow definitions may exclude AI systems that pose genuine risks. The OECD's updated definition (revised in 2024) attempts to address this by focusing on systems that can, for a given set of objectives, generate outputs such as predictions, recommendations, or decisions.

### Jurisdictional Fragmentation

The lack of a single global AI governance framework means that organizations operating internationally must comply with a patchwork of national and regional regulations. The EU AI Act, US executive orders, China's AI regulations, and other national frameworks impose different (and sometimes conflicting) requirements.

### Enforcement

Even where regulations exist, enforcement remains challenging. AI systems are complex, opaque, and rapidly evolving, making it difficult for regulators to assess compliance. The technical expertise required for AI oversight is in short supply within most government agencies.

### Balancing Innovation and Safety

Governance frameworks must avoid stifling beneficial innovation while effectively managing risks. Different stakeholders prioritize these objectives differently: technology companies tend to emphasize innovation, civil society groups tend to emphasize safety and rights, and governments must balance both.

### Global South Participation

Many AI governance discussions have been dominated by developed countries and large technology companies. Ensuring meaningful participation from the Global South in shaping AI governance is increasingly recognized as essential for creating frameworks that are genuinely inclusive and equitable.
